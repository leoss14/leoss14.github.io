Scratch: testing the proposed restructure

Seven hypotheses about what's wrong with the current central-bank page, each run against the actual data (N=833, 1956-07 to 2025-12). The point: see what survives a quick test before rewriting anything. Run with code/scratch_hypothesis_tests.py. This file is not linked from anywhere.

Bottom line

Two of my critiques don't survive contact with the data. Two get stronger. The single most interesting result came from a hypothesis I added halfway through (H6, the counterfactual rule).

  1. H1 (NAIRU drives the 1980 break) is mostly wrong. The Chow break at 1979-08 is highly significant under fixed 5% (F=27.9), CBO NAIRU (F=18.1), and no gap at all (F=14.7). NAIRU softens the break but doesn't kill it. The current page's claim that the break "disappears" with NAIRU is overstated.
  2. H2 (compositional persistence) is the cleanest result on the page. AC(12) collapses from 0.73 unconditional to 0.28 inside the low regime. Keep this front and centre.
  3. H3 (forecast horse race is noise) is wrong. Properly-baselined R² (vs expanding mean) is 0.95 at 1m and 0.50 at 12m. The page's reported R² = -27 numbers must come from a different baseline; either way, the models do have signal. Re-investigate before redrawing the chart.
  4. H4 (real vs expected flips the Taylor principle) doesn't survive specification change. Static OLS gives high-regime 1+aπ = 0.81 realized, 0.56 expected, both below 1. The page's NLS-with-smoothing gives 1.20/1.42 (satisfied). The finding is real but extremely specification-dependent.
  5. H5 (regime conditioning vs date split) is the most important methodological finding. A 1979-08 date split gives expected 1+aπ = 1.63 (Volcker break clearly satisfies Taylor). The regime-conditional version gives 0.56. The two units of analysis give opposite conclusions. The current page uses the one that obscures the conventional story.
  6. H6 (counterfactual rule) is the page's missing payoff. A Taylor rule estimated on 1985-2007 prescribes rates 5.7pp above what Burns set in 1972-75, 1.1pp below what Volcker set in 1979-82, and 5.7pp above what Powell set in 2021-23. The 2021-23 Fed looks numerically identical to the 1970s Fed under this benchmark. That's the story.
  7. H7 (LORO is orphaned) holds. Mean AUC 0.79. The work is decent but unrelated to the monetary policy thesis. Cut.

H1 · Does NAIRU choice make the 1980 break disappear?

Chow break test at 1979-08 under three unemployment-gap specifications

Mostly rejected
Page claim
"When the unemployment gap is measured against CBO's time-varying natural rate rather than a fixed 5%, no candidate break date exceeds the statistical significance threshold."
Test
Static OLS Taylor: i = c + (1+aπ)π + a_u · gap. Chow test compares pre-1979-08 and post-1979-08 subsamples. Three gap definitions: fixed (u − 5), CBO (u − NROU), and a no-gap variant.
SpecificationChow Fp-valueVerdict
Fixed 5% unemployment gap27.94<0.0001Significant break
CBO NAIRU gap18.09<0.0001Significant break
No unemployment gap14.67<0.0001Significant break
What this means. CBO NAIRU reduces the F-statistic by ~35% but doesn't push the p-value anywhere near non-significance. The break is robust to gap measurement. The page is making a plausible-sounding claim that the data doesn't support cleanly. The sup-Wald test on the original page may use a stricter threshold and so still report "no break", but the simple Chow says the break is real.

H2 · Is inflation persistence compositional?

Within-regime vs between-regime decomposition of AC(12)

Supported
Page claim
"Within the low-inflation regime, AC(12) drops to 0.28. Roughly 40-60% of the apparent persistence comes from mixing the two distributions, not from inflation actually being predictable within either one."
Test
Compute AC(12) on full sample and within each regime. Decompose total variance into within-regime component (E[Var(π|r)]) and between-regime component (Var(E[π|r])).
SeriesAC(12)Variance contribution
Full sample (N=833)0.7317.54
Low-inflation regime (N=622)0.2834.22 (within)
High-inflation regime (N=211)0.50512.67 (between, >100% as regimes are persistent)
What this means. Strongest result. The mean shift between regimes accounts for more variance than there is total variance to explain (the within-component partially offsets it because regimes are temporally clustered, so within-regime data has below-sample variance). The clean takeaway: if you condition on being in a regime, inflation barely autocorrelates at 12 months in the low regime. The conventional view that inflation is "persistent" is largely a level-shift artifact.

H3 · Is the forecasting horse race noise?

Walk-forward CV at 1-month and 12-month horizons, R² vs expanding mean

Rejected
My critique
The page reports R² = -27 at 12m for pooled Ridge. That's MSE 28x worse than the historical mean, suggesting catastrophic model failure. The whole exercise looks like noise.
Test
Expanding-window walk-forward CV starting at month 120. Two models (AR with 4 inflation lags; full Ridge with all lags plus unemployment gap, term spread, fed funds, regime probability). Baseline = expanding mean.
HorizonModelR² vs expanding meanRMSE
1 monthAR(4 lags)0.9530.638
1 monthFull Ridge0.9550.625
12 monthsAR(4 lags)0.5052.092
12 monthsFull Ridge0.5292.040
What this means. The 12-month R² is not negative when baselined properly. Both models retain real signal at 12 months. The full Ridge slightly beats AR (0.53 vs 0.50), so multi-feature models do add something. The catastrophic R² numbers on the current page (down to -27) must come from a different baseline (probably the test-window mean rather than expanding mean), which is essentially a methodological mistake. Need to dig into the notebook to find the discrepancy before keeping or cutting the forecasting section.

H4 · Does realized vs expected inflation flip the Taylor principle?

Static OLS Taylor rule, four sub-samples × two inflation measures

Rejected at this specification
Page claim
"With expected inflation, the high-inflation regime satisfies the Taylor principle at 1.42; with realized inflation, it does not clearly satisfy it in either regime."
Test
Static OLS Taylor: i = c + (1+aπ)π + a_u · gap_cbo, HAC SE with 12 lags. Two inflation measures (CPI YoY vs Michigan / adaptive proxy). Three samples (full, high regime, low regime).
SampleRealized 1+aπ(SE)Expected 1+aπ(SE)
Full sample0.89(0.12)0.98(0.19)
Low inflation regime1.19(0.26)0.76(0.20)
High inflation regime0.81(0.19)0.56(0.36)
What this means. Under a clean static OLS, switching to expected inflation makes the Taylor principle worse, not better. The current page reports 1.20 (realized) and 1.42 (expected) for the high regime, using a structural NLS with smoothing parameter ρ ≈ 0.97. The structural coefficient is the long-run response, which is mathematically a much larger number than the contemporaneous OLS coefficient. So the two estimates are not directly comparable, but the page is relying on the long-run interpretation to make the headline claim. Worth flagging: the result requires the structural form. With static OLS (which is what readers would do as a check), the principle is violated in both regimes under both inflation measures.

H5 · Does regime conditioning differ from a date split?

Pre-1979 vs post-1979 OLS Taylor rule, both inflation measures

Supported (important)
My critique
Defining regimes on inflation and then estimating Taylor rules within them is partly tautological. A simple date split (pre vs post Volcker, 1979-08) should give a cleaner test of the conventional story.
SplitRealized 1+aπExpected 1+aπ
Pre-1979-08 (date)0.74 (0.04)0.76 (0.07)
Post-1979-08 (date)1.04 (0.18)1.63 (0.29)
High-inflation regime0.81 (0.19)0.56 (0.36)
Low-inflation regime1.19 (0.26)0.76 (0.20)
What this means. The conventional Volcker break shows up cleanly when split by date: 1+aπ = 0.76 pre, 1.63 post, expected inflation. That is the textbook Clarida-Gali-Gertler result. The regime-based split gives 0.56 in the high regime and 0.76 in the low, both below 1. The two units of analysis give opposite headline conclusions. The current page uses regime conditioning, which obscures the very phenomenon it's trying to discuss. Either the page should drop the Taylor-principle question and use regimes only for the persistence story, or it should switch to a date-based split for the Volcker test.

H6 · Counterfactual rule, three episodes

Taylor rule estimated on 1985-2007, applied to Burns, Volcker, and post-COVID

Supported, this is the payoff
Proposed restructure
The page's current conclusion is descriptive. A counterfactual gives it a normative anchor. Train a Taylor rule on the anchored-expectations era (1985-2007) and ask what it would have prescribed at three key inflation episodes.
Test
OLS on 1985-01 to 2007-12 (N=276): i = -0.84 + 2.01 · πe + (-0.72) · gap_cbo. Apply this rule out-of-sample to three episodes: Burns 1972-75, Volcker 1979-82, post-COVID 2021-23.
EpisodeActual FFR (mean)Rule-prescribed (mean)Gap (Actual − Rule)
Burns 1972-757.3713.07−5.69 pp
Volcker 1979-8213.8312.70+1.13 pp
Post-COVID 2021-232.267.98−5.72 pp
What this means. Under the anchored-era Taylor rule, Burns was 5.7pp too easy on average over 1972-75, Volcker was almost exactly on the rule (with a 1.1pp overshoot), and the 2021-23 Fed was 5.7pp too easy. The 2021-23 Fed looks numerically indistinguishable from the 1970s Fed under this benchmark.

That's a real claim with content, and it's the kind of thing the page should be doing. The outputs/leftovers/ folder already has counterfactual_episodes.png and counterfactual_rule_comparison.png charts that probably show exactly this. They should be central, not hidden.

Implications for the restructure

Revised proposal

The original four-conclusion structure of the page reflected genuine analytical work but tied together four largely independent results. The hypothesis tests above suggest a different shape:

Part I · Inflation regimes. Keep the Markov-switching identification (it's clean). Keep the persistence decomposition (H2 strongly supported). Drop the 4-regime non-result.

Part II · What a Taylor rule says about the modern Fed. Replace the regime-conditional Taylor rule (H5: gives opposite answer to the date split) with a clean date-based estimation on 1985-2007. Use this as the benchmark rule.

Part III · Three episodes under one rule. H6 result: Burns 1972-75, Volcker 1979-82, post-COVID 2021-23 all compared to the same rule. This is the page's payoff. The leftovers folder already has the charts.

Section IV / Limitations. Honestly state that the 1980 break does survive even with NAIRU (H1: my critique of the page was wrong, the page's claim is overstated). Note the specification-sensitivity of the Taylor principle test (H4). Note that the page does NOT use real-time data and that this matters.

Drop: LORO recession analysis (H7: separate paper), the 6-way forecast horse race (the finding is that complex models barely beat AR, that's a one-table appendix point), the 4-state Markov-switching mentions.

Script: code/scratch_hypothesis_tests.py. Data: 833 monthly obs 1956-07 to 2025-12. All numbers above came from a single run of the script; verdicts and tables are populated by hand from its stdout so there's no need to re-run to view this page. Re-run if you change the script.
