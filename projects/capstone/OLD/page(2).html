Natural Resources & Economic Complexity

Despite substantial natural wealth, resource-rich countries often remain locked into low-complexity production structures, underperforming economies with far fewer endowments. This project examines why, and which conditions allow resource-rich countries to break the pattern. Using clustering, pooled econometrics, and machine learning across 54 resource-dependent countries and 25 years of data, it identifies the structural factors that separate successful diversifiers from persistent resource dependents.

Motivation

Research Questions

The project addresses two primary questions. First, how successful have resource-rich emerging economies been at developing value-added industries from natural resource extraction? Second, what factors explain the divergence across countries? Two secondary questions follow: what are the implications of slow diversification for fiscal revenues, export structure, and sovereign credit quality, and what policy levers and institutional conditions support or hinder the transition?

The Resource Curse

The literature finds weak evidence for the resource curse as a deterministic mechanism. The central question has shifted from whether resources harm growth to under what conditions they do. Mehlum, Moene & Torvik (2006) show that resources impede growth only where institutions are “grabber-friendly”; where rule of law is strong, resources accelerate growth. Venables (2016) argues that resource-rich governments tend to convert extraction rents into consumption rather than productive investment in human capital, infrastructure, or financial assets. Van der Ploeg & Poelhekke (2009) find that resource abundance boosts growth directly but undermines it through macroeconomic volatility, shifting investment toward short-term, low-return projects. Isham et al. (2005) distinguish between point-source resources (oil, diamonds), which concentrate rents among elites, and diffuse resources (agriculture, forestry), which spread benefits more broadly.

Economic Complexity

The Economic Complexity Index (ECI), developed by the Harvard Growth Lab, measures the degree of accumulated productive “know-how” in a country’s economy. It uses trade data to compute two quantities: diversity (the number of different products a country exports competitively) and ubiquity (the number of countries that export those same products). Countries that export many products that few other countries can make score highest. Japan, Germany, and Switzerland rank at the top; many oil exporters and low-income countries rank at the bottom. The ECI serves as the dependent variable throughout the analysis.

Design

The Analytical Approach

The project combines two quantitative strategies with three qualitative case studies.

Econometrics

Pooled OLS models that test Venables’ (2016) investment hypothesis empirically, using an interaction strategy inspired by Mehlum et al. (2006). Standard errors are clustered by country.

Machine Learning

Unsupervised techniques (PCA and K-means) group countries by resource extraction profile. Supervised models (LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net, Random Forest) take a theory-agnostic approach to identify predictors.

Case Studies

The Republic of Congo, Azerbaijan, and Chile build on the empirical findings, identify country-specific bottlenecks, and present targeted policy recommendations.

Results Preview

Three Pillars Behind Industrial Upgrading

Across both the econometric and machine learning analyses, three structural variables consistently predict sustained diversification and the development of value-added industries.

Financial Depth

Domestic Credit to Private Sector

Where firms cannot borrow, resource rents stay in the extractive sector rather than financing new industries. Domestic credit emerges as the single strongest predictor in the ML models.

Energy Infrastructure

Access to Electricity

Manufacturing requires reliable electricity. Countries with low electrification rates cannot sustain industrial production outside extraction. Electricity access is a consistent predictor across all ML specifications.

Human Capital

Education & Health Index

Without an educated and healthy workforce, countries remain locked into the lowest segments of the value chain. Human capital is positive and significant across all econometric specifications.

Data

Panel Construction

The sample covers 54 resource-rich developing economies (natural resource rents ≥ 5% of GDP in 1995) observed over 1995 to 2019, drawing on six primary sources: World Bank WDI, IMF WEO and Capital Stock datasets, V-Dem governance indicators, Penn World Table 10.01, CEPII GeoDist, and commodity production data from the Energy Institute, USGS, and Our World in Data. 20+ commodities are aggregated into four categories (oil, natural gas, coal, metals) and converted to dollar values using a consolidated price file. Missing values are filled by linear interpolation (capped at three years) followed by K-Nearest Neighbours imputation (k=5, distance-weighted).

Country Clustering

Not all resource-rich countries are alike. To compare countries against structural peers, we use PCA to compress 21 resource production variables into two dimensions, then K-means clustering (k=6) to group countries by extraction profile. Countries within the same cluster share similar combinations of resource dependence and economic sophistication.

Extreme oil dependence (>8% of GDP), very low economic complexity, weak education and health outcomes. The classic resource curse profile.

Cluster Transitions (1995–2019)

Do countries stay in the same group over time? The Sankey diagram traces movements between clusters from 1995 to 2019. Of 126 countries tracked across both years, 100 (79%) remained in their original cluster. The remaining 26 shifted. Several patterns stand out.

Transition Patterns

  • Upward mobility is possible but uncommon: 12 countries moved from Low-Income Diversified to Advanced Economies, including Brazil, Malaysia, Thailand, Türkiye, and South Africa. These transitions accompanied sustained manufacturing development and human capital accumulation.
  • Mining intensification: 6 countries moved into Mining-Dependent status (Burkina Faso, Bolivia, DRC, Ghana, Mali, Peru), reflecting expanded metals extraction relative to GDP.

Part III

Econometric Results

The econometric analysis tests Venables’ (2016) hypothesis that resource-rich countries underperform because they convert extraction rents into consumption rather than productive investment. The identification strategy follows Mehlum et al. (2006): rather than estimating a direct resource effect, we interact resource production with human capital (HCI) and physical capital investment (GFCF) to test whether investment channels moderate the resource curse.

All specifications use pooled OLS on the 54-country sample with standard errors clustered at the country level (54 groups). The dependent variable is the raw ECI. Variables entering as interactions are mean-centred (grand mean) before multiplication to reduce collinearity.

Core Specification

$$\begin{aligned} ECI_{it} = \;\beta_0 &+ \beta_1 \text{HCI}_{it} + \beta_2 \text{GFCF}_{it} + \beta_3 \text{Prod}_{it} + \beta_4 \text{Forest}_{it} + \boldsymbol{\gamma}' \mathbf{X}_{it} \\ &+ \beta_8 (\text{HCI}_{it} \times \text{Prod}_{it}) + \beta_9 (\text{GFCF}_{it} \times \text{Prod}_{it}) \\ &+ \beta_{10} (\text{HCI}_{it} \times \text{Forest}_{it}) + \beta_{11} (\text{GFCF}_{it} \times \text{Forest}_{it}) \\ &+ \beta_{12} ECI_{i,t-1} + \epsilon_{it} \end{aligned}$$

where \(\mathbf{X}_{it}\) = Political Stability, Rule of Law, Trade Openness. Model 3a omits \(ECI_{i,t-1}\); Model 3b includes it.

Core Specifications (Models 1–3)

Model 1: pooled OLS with 44 regressors (sign-checking reference only). Model 2: autoregressive baseline (lagged ECI only, R² > 0.97). Models 3a–3e: four interaction terms; 3a without lag, 3b with lag, 3c all regressors lagged one period, 3d log-transformed ECI, 3e first-differenced ECI.

Extended Specifications (Models 4–6)

Models 4a/4b: add resource-type dummies (hydrocarbons, subsoil metals, precious metals) and access to electricity. Model 5: AR baseline on log(ECI). Model 6: first-difference specification with error-correction term and extended controls.

Key Findings

Human Capital (HCI) is positive and significant across all specifications, indicating that HCI contributes to complexity growth beyond a country’s existing trajectory. Gross Fixed Capital Formation is also positive and significant. Production Value carries a negative coefficient but is generally not significant, consistent with the conditional curse view: resources are not inherently harmful, but their effect depends on how rents are channelled. Institutional controls (rule of law, political stability) lose significance once lagged ECI is included, suggesting their effect operates through long-run structural channels rather than year-on-year variation.

Transition

Why Machine Learning after Econometrics?

Econometric models select variables guided by theory. This ensures interpretability but risks omitting predictors that matter empirically. With 48 available variables across nine thematic groups, a theory-driven specification can only test a small subset. Important factors may be missed simply because they fall outside the theoretical framework.

The ML pipeline addresses this by feeding all available variables into the models and letting the algorithms identify which predictors carry the most explanatory power. Regularisation techniques (LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net) penalise weak coefficients, shrinking them toward zero. Random Forest calculates feature importance by capturing nonlinear relationships. Where ML and econometrics converge on the same predictors, confidence in those variables is strengthened. Where they diverge, ML highlights factors the theory-driven model may have overlooked.

Part IV

Machine Learning Insights

The ML pipeline uses a temporal train/test split: training on 1995 through 2014, testing on 2015 through 2019. Cross-validation uses PanelTemporalCV, an expanding-window scheme with five splits and a one-year gap, preventing temporal leakage. The feature set comprises 24 variables: 19 base features, three engineered features (five-year rolling means of inflation and real interest rates, plus a resource rent Herfindahl index), and two interaction terms (HCI × Production, GFCF × Production). Two targets are modelled: ECI in levels and year-on-year change (ΔECI).

Convergence and Divergence

Convergence: Human Capital and the HCI × Production interaction rank as top predictors in both the econometric and ML approaches. Divergence: Domestic credit to the private sector and electricity access are the strongest ML predictors of ECI, factors absent from the theory-driven regression specification. This is the central value of the ML pipeline: it identifies structural variables that the econometric framework, constrained by Venables’ investment hypothesis, does not test directly.

Part V

Projected ECI Trajectories (2020–2030)

All models are retrained on the full 1995–2019 sample. Features are extrapolated using the last-five-year linear trend per country, and predictions are generated iteratively (each year’s lagged ECI equals the prior year’s prediction). The ensemble forecast averages across all level and accumulated-Δ predictions. These projections carry substantial uncertainty: they assume structural trends continue linearly over an eleven-year horizon, with no prediction intervals attached.

Improving

Chile: Strong macroeconomic stability and institutions. Green energy abundance provides a potential base for green industrialisation. Mongolia: Heavy investment in education and financial infrastructure alongside mining-linked industrialisation.

Declining

Saudi Arabia: Private credit remains heavily concentrated in real estate rather than productive sectors. Kazakhstan: Persistent petroleum dependence has worsened, and lagging human capital continues to erode possible complexity gains.

Comparative Case Studies

Each case study illustrates a different bottleneck. The binding constraint shifts as countries grow richer: in low-income settings, all three pillars fail simultaneously; in middle-income countries, one pillar becomes the binding constraint.

Republic of Congo

Oil accounts for roughly 40% of GDP, 80% of exports, and 60% of government revenues. Only 50% of the population has access to electricity and 13% of roads are paved. 94% of active firms are informal, cutting them off from formal credit and upskilling. Only 18% of adults hold a formal bank account. Education quality is very low: only 60% of children completing primary school progress to secondary, and the country has around 23,000 teachers against an estimated need of 48,000.

Azerbaijan

Over 45% of what the government classifies as “non-oil” manufacturing is petroleum-related. Infrastructure spending is concentrated on pipelines. SOFAZ (the sovereign wealth fund) transfers to the budget have at times exceeded the fund’s own investment returns. Politically connected contracts, opaque procurement, and SOFAZ’s lack of independence from presidential orders enable rent capture. Non-performing loans reached 21% after the 2015–2016 banking crisis.

Chile

Chile has the strongest institutions in Latin America, yet its ECI has declined since the mid-2000s. R&D spending at 0.34% of GDP is one-sixth of the OECD average. PISA maths scores are 60 points below the OECD average, and worker productivity is 50% below the OECD average. Chilean exports sit in the periphery of the product space, meaning they connect to few other, more complex products. 54% of copper exports go to China for downstream processing rather than being refined domestically.

Synthesis

Conclusions and Implications

No single policy lever is sufficient across all settings. The relative importance of financial depth, infrastructure, and human capital shifts with institutional and macroeconomic conditions. The three pillars provide a framework for identifying which constraint is binding in each country context.

01

Financial Depth

Domestic credit to the private sector emerged as the single strongest ML predictor. In Congo, only a minority of firms access financing, increasing barriers to firm formalisation. Expanding bank financing is a key precondition for oil revenues to reach the productive economy.

02

Infrastructure & Governance

Electricity access is a consistent predictor across all ML models, but infrastructure spending only translates into diversification when governance channels it beyond the resource sector. In Azerbaijan, redirecting investment toward petrochemical processing and renewable energy engineering would target sectors adjacent to the existing export basket.

03

Human Capital

Positive and significant across all econometric specifications. Chile has the strongest institutions in Latin America, yet its ECI has declined since the mid-2000s. Directing lithium revenues toward technical training and applied research would build the workforce needed for downstream processing.

04

Different starting points need different strategies

The six-cluster typology and the three case studies confirm that the binding constraint shifts as countries develop. Petrostates face institutional and labour-market reforms; mining-dependent economies need downstream processing; middle-income countries with strong institutions need R&D investment and education quality improvements.

Methodological Notes

Data Processing

20+ commodities aggregated into four categories (oil, natural gas, coal, metals). Production values: physical quantity × market price (USD). GDP normalisation: Production Value / GDP × 100. All features standardised (mean=0, SD=1) before PCA, clustering, and regularised regression. 54-country sample defined by NR rents ≥ 5% of GDP in 1995. Linear interpolation (capped 3 years) + KNN imputation (k=5).

Statistical Methods

PCA: 2 components, 55.7% variance. K-means: k=6, silhouette=0.344. Regressions: pooled OLS, 54 countries, clustered SE; Models 1–6 including interaction models (3a–3e), extended controls (4a/4b), first-difference (6). ML: temporal split (train ≤2014, test 2015–2019), LASSO/Ridge/ElasticNet/RF/XGBoost, PanelTemporalCV (expanding-window, 5 splits, 1-year gap), 24 features, two targets (ECI level + ΔECI). Bootstrap: 200 country-block resamples.

Full code and data pipeline available on GitHub or on request.

Robustness Checks

Forest Rent Exclusion

The World Bank’s total natural resource rents variable includes forest rents, inflating resource-dependence scores for 26 of the 54 countries with significant forestry sectors. A corrected pipeline subtracts forest and coal rents, re-applies a 3% threshold (with Gulf states guaranteed), yielding a 38-country sample. All core models are re-estimated on this and three intermediate samples, with HAC-panel standard errors (Bartlett kernel, 3 lags) as a more conservative alternative.
