Beyond the Resource Curse

Project Overview

Research Question

Do natural resources constrain or enable economic diversification? Among developing economies, which structural conditions allow resource exporters to build value-added industries, and how does the binding constraint vary by country? The analysis tests whether the resource curse operates uniformly or whether its severity depends on the type of resource, the strength of institutions, and on whether human capital investment can overcome the structural disadvantages of resource dependence.

Analytical Approach

The project combines two quantitative strategies with three qualitative case studies. Each strategy answers a different question, and the case studies illustrate how the binding constraint varies by country context.

Unsupervised Learning

PCA reduces the data to its main dimensions; K-means assigns each country to one of four clusters based on its resource and development profile, providing structural peer groups for comparison.

Panel Econometrics

Fixed-effects panel regression estimates within-country relationships between resource production and economic complexity. Country and year fixed effects absorb unobserved heterogeneity; standard errors clustered at the country level.

Machine Learning

Seven algorithms (regularised linear models and tree-based ensembles) test how well resource and institutional variables predict complexity out of sample. SHAP decomposes predictions to identify which variables carry the most weight.

Three Structural Pillars

Across the econometric and machine learning analyses, three structural variables consistently predict gains in economic complexity: domestic credit to the private sector (financial depth), access to electricity (energy infrastructure), and the Human Capital Index (workforce education and health). Resource intensity, on its own, is associated with lower complexity in the headline regression and does not predict gains in either approach. Investment, infrastructure, and education are the channels that allow resource-rich economies to convert extraction rents into productive capacity.

Sample and Coverage

Panel Construction

The dataset spans 1995 to 2019 and draws on six primary sources: World Bank World Development Indicators, IMF World Economic Outlook and Capital Stock, V-Dem governance indicators, Penn World Table 10.01, CEPII GeoDist, and commodity production data (Energy Institute, USGS, Our World in Data). The fixed-effects panel regression and the ML pipeline use a balanced panel of 107 developing economies, defined by the 1995 World Bank high-income filter with the six Gulf states force-included. The cluster analysis is fit on a tighter 56-country resource-dependent subsample (forest-adjusted natural resource rents above 1% of GDP in 1995). Imputation procedure, commodity aggregation, and the full sample-definition criteria are detailed in the data appendix.

Clustering

Country Clustering

Resource-dependent economies vary widely: petrostates with minimal manufacturing, mining-led exporters with growing industrial bases, and diversified producers operating at moderate scale across multiple commodities. The 56 countries with adjusted natural resource rents above 1% of GDP in 1995 are grouped by similarity across three subsoil rent shares (oil, gas, mineral, all as % of GDP) and log production per capita. K-means with k=4 is fit on country-level means across the 1995-2019 panel, so cluster assignments reflect each country’s structural resource profile rather than a single year’s outcome. The remaining 51 panel countries are tagged “Non-resource-dependent”. Cluster names are derived from each centroid; full diagnostics are in the appendix.

Diversified Producers (29)

Argentina, China, Dominican Republic, India, Indonesia, Malaysia, Mexico, South Africa. Modest resource intensity across multiple categories (total natural-resource rents averaging about 6% of GDP); growing manufacturing sectors and middle-income status.

Oil Exporters (15)

Angola, Iran, Iraq, Kazakhstan, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Venezuela. Oil-dominated rent profile (oil rents averaging about 30% of GDP, gas share negligible); wide spread of institutional strength within the group.

Petrostates (6)

Algeria, Bahrain, Qatar, Russia, Trinidad and Tobago, Uzbekistan. Mixed hydrocarbon profile with a substantial natural-gas component alongside oil (gas rents averaging about 5% of GDP, the highest of any cluster); total hydrocarbon rents around 20% of GDP.

Mining Exporters (6)

Chile, Guinea, Mauritania, Mongolia, Papua New Guinea, Peru. Metals and minerals dominate the rent profile (mineral rents averaging about 7% of GDP); downstream processing varies substantially within the group.

Analysis

Econometric Results

The econometric strategy tests Venables’ (2016) hypothesis that resource-rich countries underperform because they convert extraction rents into consumption rather than productive investment. Following Mehlum, Moene & Torvik (2006), the specification interacts resource production with the two main investment channels (human capital and physical capital formation) rather than estimating a direct resource effect. Country and year fixed effects absorb time-invariant country characteristics and global commodity-price shocks; standard errors are clustered at the country level.

Core Specification

$$\begin{aligned} ECI_{it} = \;\beta_0 &+ \beta_1 \text{HCI}_{it} + \beta_2 \text{GFCF}_{it} + \beta_3 \text{Prod}_{it} + \boldsymbol{\gamma}' \mathbf{X}_{it} \\ &+ \beta_4 (\text{HCI}_{it} \times \text{Prod}_{it}) + \beta_5 (\text{GFCF}_{it} \times \text{Prod}_{it}) \\ &+ \beta_6 ECI_{i,t-1} + \epsilon_{it} \end{aligned}$$

where \(\mathbf{X}_{it}\) = Political Stability, Rule of Law, Trade Openness.

Variable Groups

Natural Resources: subsoil production value.

Investment: Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF), Human Capital Index (HCI).

Institutions: rule of law, political stability.

Economic: trade openness, lagged ECI.

Identification Strategy

The specification follows Mehlum, Moene & Torvik (2006) in interacting resource production with the two main investment channels (human capital and physical capital formation) rather than estimating a direct resource effect alone. The interaction terms test whether the marginal return to investment in human or physical capital is higher in resource-intensive settings.

Key Findings

The headline specification (Model 3b) on the 107-country panel produces four significant coefficients. The CI column reports the 95% confidence interval around the point estimate.

Variable β 95% CI Reading
Human Capital (HCI) +0.19 [0.06, 0.32] Positive and significant; HCI retains a meaningful effect even with lagged ECI in the specification.
Capital Formation (GFCF) +0.020 [0.006, 0.033] Positive and significant.
Rule of Law +0.06 [0.01, 0.11] Positive and significant. Political stability is positive but does not reach significance.
Production Value −0.007 [−0.014, −0.0003] Negative and significant. Holding capital fixed, extractive intensity is associated with lower complexity, the direct empirical signal of the resource curse that earlier samples lacked the statistical power to identify.

Both interaction terms (HCI × Production, GFCF × Production) are close to zero and not statistically distinguishable from zero. The conditional-curse reading therefore rests on the main effects rather than on moderation: extractive intensity depresses complexity on average, while human capital, physical capital, and institutional quality each contribute independently.

Analysis

Machine Learning

The econometric specification above is theory-driven: variables are chosen to test a particular causal hypothesis, and the regression reports within-country partial effects. The ML pipeline serves a different purpose. It accepts all 20 available structural variables, applies regularisation (LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net) and a non-linear ensemble (Random Forest), and asks which features carry the most out-of-sample predictive content for ECI. Where the two approaches converge, the variable is doubly identified; where they diverge, ML flags factors outside the theoretical specification that nonetheless predict complexity.

Convergence: human capital

Human Capital is positive and significant in the panel regression, sits in the top three of the regularised regression coefficients (after the autoregressive baseline), and contributes meaningfully to Random Forest importance. Regression and ML point at the same variable from different statistical angles.

Divergence: financial depth and electricity access

Domestic credit to the private sector and access to electricity emerge as strong ML predictors that the theory-driven regression specification does not test. Random Forest places Domestic Credit first among policy variables. The regularised regressions place Electricity higher than Domestic Credit by mean absolute coefficient (0.041 vs 0.027), with both clearly above the rest of the macro feature set.

Interaction terms shrunk to zero under LASSO

HCI × Production and GFCF × Production are heavily shrunk by LASSO and carry little out-of-sample predictive content, consistent with the regression finding that they are not statistically distinguishable from zero.

Coefficient Agreement: LASSO, Ridge, and Elastic Net

Standardised coefficients from three regularised linear models on the same feature set. Bars to the right of zero predict higher ECI; bars to the left predict lower ECI. Agreement across the three models is a stability check.

The Random Forest is a different kind of model: instead of fitting a single equation, it averages predictions from hundreds of decision trees, each trained on a random subset of countries and variables. The chart below ranks variables by how much they reduce prediction error across the ensemble. Unlike the regularised regressions, Random Forest captures non-linear effects (where a variable matters more in some ranges than others) and interactions automatically, without having to specify them in advance. The autoregressive lag of ECI dominates because past complexity is a strong predictor of next-year complexity. Among policy variables, Domestic Credit, Electricity Access, and Human Capital consistently rank near the top, mirroring the regularised regression and the headline panel result.

Forecast

Projected ECI Trajectories to 2030

Based on current predictor levels, the ensemble model projects which countries are positioned for ECI gains and which are positioned for losses through 2030. Forecasts assume that each country’s structural variables continue along their last-five-year linear trend; predictions are generated iteratively, with each year’s lagged ECI equal to the prior year’s prediction. These projections carry substantial uncertainty over an eleven-year horizon.

Republic of Congo

Oil accounts for roughly 40% of GDP, 80% of exports, and 60% of government revenues. Only 50% of the population has access to electricity and 13% of roads are paved. 94% of active firms are informal, cutting them off from formal credit and upskilling. Only 18% of adults hold a formal bank account. Education quality is very low: only 60% of children completing primary school progress to secondary, and the country has around 23,000 teachers against an estimated need of 48,000.

Republic of the Congo: oil fields and the road network

Offshore production around Pointe-Noire alongside the documented road network, classified by surface quality.

Policy Recommendations

Education investment. Delink education spending from oil revenue cycles through a dedicated fund mechanism within the CEMAC framework. Shift focus from broad-based low-capacity schooling to continuous student progression and quality.

Open the electricity sector to private participation. The 2003 Electricity Code already provides the institutional framework; political will to enforce it is needed.

Strengthen oil revenue transparency through independent verification systems and production reporting.

Azerbaijan

Over 45% of what the government classifies as “non-oil” manufacturing is petroleum-related. Infrastructure spending is concentrated on pipelines. SOFAZ (the sovereign wealth fund) transfers to the budget have at times exceeded the fund’s own investment returns. Politically connected contracts, opaque procurement, and SOFAZ’s lack of independence from presidential orders enable rent capture. The State Statistical Committee does not publish a Gini coefficient. Non-performing loans reached 21% after the 2015–2016 banking crisis.

Azerbaijan: oil and gas infrastructure

Caspian extraction sites and the six export pipelines connecting Azerbaijani fields to Black Sea, Mediterranean and Adriatic terminals.

Policy Recommendations

Mitigate rent capture. Establish independent oversight bodies for sovereign wealth fund governance, separating SOFAZ disbursement decisions from presidential authority.

Redirect capital toward adjacent sectors. Prioritise investment in sectors closely related to the existing export basket (petrochemical processing, renewable energy engineering) rather than reinforcing extractive capacity.

Establish independent data institutions. An autonomous national statistics agency, with consistent publication of non-oil deficit projections and SOFAZ sustainability assessments.

Chile

Chile has the strongest institutions in Latin America, yet its ECI has declined since the mid-2000s. R&D spending at 0.34% of GDP is one-sixth of the OECD average. PISA maths scores are 60 points below the OECD average, and worker productivity is 50% below the OECD average. Chilean exports sit in the periphery of the product space, meaning they connect to few other, more complex products. 54% of copper exports go to China for downstream processing rather than being refined domestically. With infrastructure and credit access at near-OECD levels, the binding constraint is human capital quality and the absence of an R&D ecosystem to anchor downstream processing.

Chile: copper, lithium and the mineral supply chain

Production sites, processing plants and Pacific export ports across the northern mining corridor; switch to the Exports view to trace shipments from each port to 70 destination markets.

Policy Recommendations

Direct lithium revenues toward education quality and applied research, allocating a defined share of rents for technical training institutes and applied research, following Norway’s SINTEF model.

Build the domestic mining supply chain through procurement preferences for domestic suppliers and dedicated credit lines with risk-sharing facilities.

Invest in logistics infrastructure along the northern mining corridor: port expansion, rail gauge standardisation, and energy connections to support proposed lithium processing facilities and reduce freight costs.

Synthesis

Conclusions and Implications

No single policy lever is sufficient across all settings. The relative importance of financial depth, infrastructure, and human capital shifts with institutional and macroeconomic conditions. The three pillars provide a framework for identifying which constraint is binding in each country context.

01

Financial Depth

Domestic credit to the private sector ranks first among policy variables on Random Forest importance and remains stable in sign across LASSO bootstrap iterations. In Congo, only a minority of firms have access to financing, which increases barriers to firm formalisation. Expanding bank financing is a precondition for resource revenues to reach the productive economy.

02

Infrastructure & Governance

Electricity access is a consistent predictor across all ML models, but infrastructure spending only translates into diversification when governance channels it beyond the resource sector. In Azerbaijan, redirecting investment toward petrochemical processing and renewable energy engineering would target sectors adjacent to the existing export basket.

03

Human Capital

Positive and significant across all econometric specifications. Chile has the strongest institutions in Latin America, yet its ECI has declined since the mid-2000s. Directing lithium revenues toward technical training and applied research, following Norway’s SINTEF model, would build the workforce needed for downstream processing.

04

Different Starting Points Need Different Strategies

The four-cluster typology and the three case studies confirm that the binding constraint shifts as countries develop. Congo faces failures in all three pillars simultaneously; Azerbaijan faces a governance and Dutch Disease problem; Chile has the institutions and infrastructure but lacks the human capital quality and R&D base to move into downstream processing. Structural change requires sustained, long-horizon interventions.

Robustness

Sample Sensitivity

The 107-country headline sample applies only the high-income filter (with Gulf force-included), so it covers the full spectrum from non-resource economies to petrostates. To confirm that the main findings do not depend on this broad construction, the headline specification is re-estimated on three tighter sample definitions, retaining only countries with adjusted natural resource rents above 1%, 2%, and 3% of GDP in 1995. HAC-panel standard errors (Bartlett kernel, 3 lags) are also reported as a more conservative inference alternative.

Findings That Survive

The negative coefficient on Production Value survives across all four sample definitions. Point estimates move between −0.007 and −0.014 but remain negative throughout, identifying a robust within-country signal: more extractive intensity is associated with lower complexity, holding investment and institutions fixed.

Human Capital is positive across all samples. Its confidence interval widens as the sample tightens (fewer countries, less precise estimate); it stays significant on the headline panel and is borderline at the 1% subsample.

Capital Formation is positive and significant in every sample, with stable magnitude.

Neither interaction term (HCI × Production, GFCF × Production) reaches significance in any of the four samples. The conditional-curse mechanism in this dataset operates through main effects rather than statistically detectable moderation.

Appendix

Methodology and Supplementary Material

Concepts and Literature Background

Economic complexity measures how sophisticated and diverse a country’s exports are. Countries that produce many different, hard-to-make products (pharmaceuticals, machinery, electronics) score high on the Economic Complexity Index (ECI). Countries whose exports are dominated by raw materials score low. Japan, Germany, and Switzerland rank highest; many oil exporters and low-income countries rank lowest.

The resource curse is the observation that countries rich in natural resources often grow more slowly, have weaker institutions, and develop less diverse economies than resource-poor countries. Nigeria and Venezuela are classic examples: abundant natural wealth, low economic complexity. Norway, Canada, and Australia show that resource wealth and economic sophistication can coexist.

The empirical literature finds weak evidence for the curse as a deterministic mechanism. Mehlum, Moene & Torvik (2006) show that resources impede growth only where institutions are “grabber-friendly”; where rule of law is strong, resources accelerate growth. Venables (2016) argues that resource-rich governments tend to convert extraction rents into consumption rather than productive investment. The central question has shifted from whether resources harm growth to under what conditions they do.

Human capital, measured by the World Bank’s Human Capital Index (HCI), captures education and health outcomes in a country’s workforce. It is one of the strongest predictors of economic complexity, and this project tests whether its effect is weakened in countries where natural resources dominate the economy.

Methodology and Estimation

Data Processing

20+ commodities aggregated into four categories (oil, natural gas, coal, metals). Production values computed as physical quantity × market price (USD). All features standardised (mean=0, SD=1) before PCA, clustering, and regularised regression. Missing values handled by linear interpolation (capped at three years) followed by KNN imputation (k=5, distance-weighted). The 107-country headline sample for regression and ML applies the 1995 high-income filter with the six Gulf states force-included. The 56-country clustering subsample additionally requires forest-adjusted natural resource rents above 1% of GDP in 1995. The Robustness section reports estimates on three tighter sample definitions.

Statistical Methods

PCA: 2 components, 50.0% variance explained (PC1 26.9%, PC2 23.1%). K-means: k=4 on country panel means across 1995-2019, using oil, gas, and mineral rent shares plus log production per capita. Panel regression: two-way fixed effects (country and year) with country-clustered standard errors. The headline specification includes lagged ECI and two interaction terms (HCI × Prod, GFCF × Prod). ML pipeline: regularised regression (LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net) and Random Forest on 20 structural features, with cross-validation and a temporal train/test split. Bootstrap inference: 200 country-block resamples.

Full code and data pipeline available on GitHub or on request.

Data Sources and Sample Construction

Missing values are filled by linear interpolation (capped at three years) followed by K-Nearest Neighbours imputation (k=5, distance-weighted). 20+ commodities are aggregated into four categories (oil, natural gas, coal, metals) and converted to dollar values using a consolidated price file.

Sample definition (full criteria)

The headline sample for regression and ML is selected on two criteria applied to the 1995 snapshot. First, countries classified as high-income by the World Bank in 1995 (Norway, Australia, Canada, and similar advanced economies) are excluded, restricting the analysis to developing economies. Second, the six Gulf states (Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, United Arab Emirates) are force-included regardless of income status, since several were high-income in 1995 and their oil sectors come online later in the panel. No rent-share threshold is applied, so the sample spans the full range from non-resource economies (Korea, Bangladesh, Czechia) to petrostates (Saudi Arabia, Qatar).

The clustering exercise uses a tighter 56-country subsample defined by an additional criterion: forest-adjusted natural resource rents above 1% of GDP in 1995. Forest rents are subtracted from the World Bank total because diffuse forestry rents enter the resource share through a different economic channel than point-source extractive rents (oil, gas, minerals) and would otherwise inflate the apparent dependence of countries whose rent base is timber rather than subsoil. Countries in the 107-panel that fail this criterion appear in the typology with the label "Non-resource-dependent" rather than being forced into the nearest k-means centroid.

Supplementary Data

Resource Diversity & Intensity Indices

Diversity uses Shannon entropy to measure how many different types of resources a country produces at a globally significant level. It is highest when production is spread evenly across categories (fossil fuels, base metals, precious metals, battery/strategic metals, industrial minerals) and lowest when concentrated in just one. Only resources where the country holds at least 0.5% of global production are counted. A power transformation (x1.5) rewards market dominance: holding 20% of global cobalt production counts for more than holding 2% of global coal.

Intensity measures total resource production value as a share of GDP. Values range from near-zero for service economies to above 15% for Kuwait and Congo Republic.

Principal Component Analysis

PCA is computed on ten country-level variables describing each economy’s resource and structural profile: production value per capita, total natural-resource rents (% of GDP), oil rents, natural-gas rents, mineral rents and forestry rents (each as % of GDP), GDP per capita, trade openness, manufacturing share, and services share. The first two components together explain about 50% of total variation in this feature set. PCA is used for visualisation only; the clustering in the main analysis is fit on its own narrower feature set (oil, gas, and mineral rent shares plus log production per capita), not on the PCA components.

PC1: Resource Dependence (~27%)

Loads positively on total natural-resource rents, oil rents, and production value; loads negatively on manufacturing and services shares. Separates economies whose output is dominated by extractive sectors from those with more diversified production bases.

PC2: Development Scale (~23%)

Loads positively on production value, GDP per capita, trade openness, and services; loads negatively on forestry rents. Separates larger, more open economies from low-income, forestry-reliant ones, cutting across the resource-dependence axis above.
