Capstone with Moody's Ratings

Beyond the Resource Curse

Do natural resources constrain or enable economic diversification? The project tests this on a 73-country panel from 1995 to 2024, combining panel fixed-effects regressions, k-means clustering on commodity export profiles, and a machine learning ensemble with SHAP-based feature attribution. Case studies on the Republic of Congo, Azerbaijan, and Chile.

Project Overview

Research Question

Do natural resources constrain or enable economic diversification? Among developing economies, which structural conditions allow resource exporters to build value-added industries, and how does the binding constraint vary by country? The analysis tests whether the resource curse operates uniformly or whether its severity depends on the type of resource, the strength of institutions, and on whether human capital investment can overcome the structural disadvantages of resource dependence. The research was developed with Moody’s Ratings as part of their sovereign credit work on resource-dependent economies.

Analytical Approach

The project combines three quantitative strategies. Each strategy answers a different question, and reading them together brackets the headline finding.

Unsupervised Learning

To understand the resource profile of different extractive countries, we use export data to divide them into groups (or clusters). Principal Component Analysis (PCA) reduces the data to its main dimensions, and K-means then assigns each country to one of four clusters based on its resource and development profile.

Panel Econometrics

To estimate how resource exposure affects complexity, we run a fixed-effects panel regression on resource-exporting countries between 1995 and 2024. Country and year fixed effects absorb time-invariant heterogeneity and common shocks, so identification comes from within-country variation. Post-2019 interaction terms test for COVID-period shifts.

Machine Learning

We run a series of predictive models to test how well resource and institutional variables predict complexity out of sample. This serves two purposes: it cross-checks the panel regression using methods that do not impose a linear shape on the relationship, and it produces a tool that can forecast complexity going forward. We also report which variables carry the most weight in those predictions.

Main Findings

On a panel of 73 developing economies from 1995 to 2024, a one percentage point shift of the export basket toward natural resources is associated with a 0.0043-point fall in the Economic Complexity Index within country, controlling for global shocks (β = −0.43 per full-range share unit, significant at the 1% level). Identification comes from country-level basket shifts over time: under pooled OLS without country and year fixed effects the same coefficient collapses to −0.08 and is statistically indistinguishable from zero. In the machine learning ensemble the largest non-resource predictors of ECI are human capital and manufacturing share. Three post-2019 commodity-group interactions test for COVID-period shifts; none clears 10% significance under the headline specification.

Sample and Coverage

Panel Construction

Data are drawn from five primary sources: trade flows by commodity (UN Comtrade), economic complexity (Atlas of Economic Complexity), macroeconomic indicators (World Bank WDI and IMF WEO), human capital and capital stock (Penn World Tables), and governance (V-Dem). The 73-country sample used in the regression and ML pipelines is the subset of a 115-country candidate panel (defined by the 1995 World Bank high-income filter with the six Gulf states force-included) that has continuous Comtrade reporting across the panel window.

The cluster analysis is fit on a tighter subsample to identify production types within resource-intensive countries. This differentiates hydrocarbon-dependent countries (Qatar and much of the Gulf) from base-metals exporters (Chile, Zambia, Democratic Republic of the Congo). Sample definition, MICE imputation procedure, and commodity aggregation are detailed in the data appendix.

Clustering

Resource-Reliant Country Clusters

Resource-rich countries do not all look the same. Some run on crude oil, others on copper or iron, others on gold and diamonds, others on a mix of extractive commodities without a single dominant category. To make the variation concrete, the 64 countries whose mean resource exports exceed 40% of total exports between 1995 and 2024 are grouped by similarity across seven commodity shares (coal, crude oil, refined oil, gas, ores, base metals, precious metals). K-means with k = 4 produces the four archetypes described below.

Mixed Extractives (N = 20)

Examples include: Mongolia, Peru, Papua New Guinea, Bolivia, Mauritania, Indonesia, Egypt, South Africa. Substantial extractive exposure with no single dominant commodity; ores, precious metals, and base metals each contribute below 10% of total exports.

Hydrocarbon Petrostates (N = 23)

Examples include: Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Nigeria, Angola, Kuwait, Russia, Qatar, Azerbaijan. Crude oil dominates the export basket (around 60% of total exports on average), with smaller shares of refined oil and gas and limited downstream processing.

Base-Metals Exporters (N = 9)

Examples include: Zambia, Democratic Republic of the Congo, Chile, Mozambique, Bahrain, Montenegro, Tajikistan, Ukraine. Copper, cobalt, iron, and aluminium make up around 30% of total exports.

Precious Metals and Stones (N = 12)

Examples include: Botswana, Mali, Ghana, Guinea, Burkina Faso, Central African Republic, Suriname, Tanzania. Gold and diamonds dominate the typical export basket (around 35% of total exports on average).

Cluster Trajectories

Cluster assignments are not static across time. The four cases below show how a country's export basket can shift substantially over the panel window, well beyond short-term price swings.

Guyana moved from a gold-and-diamond exporter to a hydrocarbon exporter in the space of three years. The Stabroek block came online in December 2019; by 2022, crude oil was 87% of Guyana's total exports, displacing precious metals which had stood at 57% in 2019. Capacity at Stabroek reached around 620,000 barrels per day by the end of 2023 with the Payara project online.
Yemen lost its hydrocarbon export base after 2014. Crude and refined oil were 88% of total exports in 2010; by 2015, after the start of the civil war and the Saudi-led intervention, the share had fallen to under 1% and the Yemen LNG plant at Balhaf had shut. The 2023 basket reads as 49% precious metals, almost certainly informal gold trade in a conflict economy.
Ghana developed a hydrocarbon export stream on top of its long-standing gold base. The Jubilee field came onstream in December 2010, the TEN field in August 2016. Hydrocarbons grew from 0% of exports in 2010 to a peak of 32% in 2019, but never overtook gold; in 2023 precious metals were back to 46% of exports and hydrocarbons sat at 24%. The cluster label moves to mixed extractives because the basket is now genuinely mixed, not because oil has displaced gold.
Mozambique transitioned gradually from a base-metals economy (aluminium from the Mozal smelter, titanium from the Moma mine) to a hydrocarbon one. Onshore gas piped to South Africa from the Sasol-operated Pande and Temane fields drove hs27 to 20% of exports by 2010; the Coral South floating LNG facility, the first FLNG in Africa, started production in mid-2022 and added to that. By 2023, hydrocarbons were 58% of total exports.
Analysis

Panel Fixed-Effects Regression

The econometric strategy tests Venables’ (2016) hypothesis that resource-rich countries underperform because they convert extraction rents into consumption rather than productive investment. Country and year fixed effects absorb time-invariant country characteristics and global commodity-price shocks; standard errors are clustered at the country level. The regressor of interest is the wide resource share, constructed from trade flows as the sum of six HS27 sub-codes (coal, crude oil, refined oil, gas, ores, base metals) over total exports. Three commodity-group shares (hydrocarbons, ores, base metals) are interacted with a post-2019 indicator to test whether the COVID period shifted the relationship for specific exposures.

Core Specification

$$ECI_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta \cdot \text{Resources}_{it} + \boldsymbol{\gamma}' \mathbf{X}_{it} + \sum_{c=1}^{3} \delta_c (\text{Post2019}_t \times \text{Share}^{c}_{it}) + \epsilon_{it}$$

\(\alpha_i, \lambda_t\) are country and year fixed effects, Resources is the wide resource share, \(\mathbf{X}_{it}\) is the control vector, and \(\text{Share}^c\) is one of three commodity-group export shares.

Variable Groups

Resource exposure: wide resource share (sum of coal, crude oil, refined oil, gas, ores, base metals export shares); three post-2019 commodity-group interactions.

Investment: log Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF), log Human Capital Index (HCI).

Sectoral: Manufacturing, Industry, Services, Agriculture (each as % of GDP).

Macro: log Population, Inflation, Real interest rate, Trade openness, Domestic credit, Urban population.

Institutional: Rule of law, Political stability, Political corruption.

Fiscal: Government revenue, Primary net lending (% GDP).

Identification Strategy

Two-way fixed effects absorb time-invariant country characteristics and global commodity-price shocks. Identification of the resource coefficient comes from within-country variation in export composition over the panel window. Standard errors are clustered at the country level.

A pooled OLS specification is reported alongside as a falsification: it strips the fixed effects so the coefficient is identified on cross-sectional differences alone. A large gap between the two specifications signals that within and between variation are telling different stories.

Headline Specifications

Two specifications are reported in the main table. M1 is the headline two-way fixed effects with country-clustered standard errors. M4 is a pooled OLS without any fixed effects, included as a falsification. Three further specifications (a two-way clustering variant of M1, a parsimonious model with two resource × investment interaction terms, and a version of M1 estimated on the observed sample without the MICE imputations) are reported in the regression appendix.

COVID Interaction Terms

Three interaction terms test whether the post-2019 period changed the relationship between specific commodity-group exposures and ECI. The four HS27 sub-codes (coal, crude oil, refined oil, gas) are aggregated into a single hydrocarbons group; ores and base metals are kept separate. The motivation is empirical: hydrocarbons saw a price collapse in mid-2020 followed by an acute supply squeeze through 2022 to 2023, while base metals followed a different cycle driven by Chinese demand and battery-minerals investment. The interactions allow these channels to load differently on complexity outcomes.

A caution on the COVID interactions

The Lasso step in the ML pipeline (next section) shrinks all three interaction terms to zero under temporal cross-validation. That is consistent with the regression result that the post-2019 effects are at best borderline under M1 and do not survive a more aggressive regularisation cross-validated on held-out years. The reasonable reading is that there may be something in the COVID-period commodity data worth tracking with future panel updates, but the headline finding of the project does not depend on it.

Analysis

Machine Learning

The regression above tests specific theory: which variables enter and how identification proceeds is chosen ex ante. The ML pipeline does the inverse. It accepts the full structural feature set and asks which features carry the most out-of-sample predictive content for ECI. Convergence between the two is a sanity check; divergence flags variables that predict ECI but sit outside the theory specification.

Convergence: resource exposure pulls complexity down

The regression headline (wide resource share −0.43 within country) is reproduced in the ML pipeline: ores share, post-2019 base metals interactions, and the resource concentration index carry negative coefficients across LASSO, Ridge, and Elastic Net. The two methods agree on the direction, even when ML spreads the signal across individual commodity sub-codes rather than aggregating it.

Divergence: human capital and labour structure rank highest in ML

log Human Capital Index and Manufacturing share are the two largest positive coefficients across all three regularised linear models, and the top two features by SHAP value in XGBoost. Neither is significant in the within-country regression (M1). These are cross-country correlates: in 29 years they do not move enough within a country to be identified as within-effects, but they discriminate strongly between countries.

LASSO shrinks the COVID interactions to zero

LASSO drops post-2019 × hydrocarbons and post-2019 × ores entirely; only post-2019 × base metals survives with a small negative coefficient. This is consistent with the regression finding that none of the three interactions clears 10% significance under M1.

Coefficient Agreement: LASSO, Ridge, and Elastic Net

XGBoost is a different kind of model. Instead of fitting a single equation, it averages predictions from hundreds of gradient-boosted decision trees, capturing non-linear effects and interactions automatically. Case Studies

Comparative Case Studies

Three countries illustrate different ways the resource-curse pattern plays out in the trade data. The Republic of Congo is the canonical petrostate case: crude oil at 84% of exports and ECI deteriorating across the panel window. Azerbaijan is also a petrostate but its ECI has improved, despite wide resource share staying above 90%, suggesting movement within the hydrocarbon basket (crude to refined and gas) carries some complexity premium. Chile is the diversified resource exporter: copper and ores at roughly half of exports but the highest ECI of any country in the resource-rich subsample.

Republic of Congo

Crude oil accounts for 84% of Congo’s exports in 2023; the wide resource share is 90% and has stayed above 80% throughout the panel window. ECI fell from −0.74 in 1995 to −1.59 in 2023, one of the steepest declines in the sample. There is no visible structural diversification in the trade data: precious metals, ores, and base metals each sit at zero or near-zero across all panel years. The country is at the floor of the resource-rich ECI distribution.

Republic of the Congo: oil fields and the road network

Offshore production around Pointe-Noire alongside the documented road network, classified by surface quality.

Finding

The within-country regression (M1) attributes −0.43 ECI per unit increase in wide resource share. Congo’s wide resource share rose roughly 10 percentage points across the panel, predicting a small ECI drag from this channel alone. The observed ECI decline (−0.85 over 28 years) is much larger, consistent with multiple structural drivers (sectoral composition, institutions, fiscal capacity) shifting in the same direction at the same time. Congo is the within-country resource curse operating at full strength.

Azerbaijan

Azerbaijan’s wide resource share has stayed at 93 to 95% of total exports throughout the panel. The composition has shifted: crude oil fell from 87% of exports in 2010 to 48% in 2023, with most of the gap filled by natural gas (around 40% of total exports in 2023) and a small refined slice. ECI improved over the same window, from −0.99 in 2010 to −0.52 in 2023, despite the aggregate resource share staying essentially flat. The trade-side metric does not capture this kind of within-HS27 diversification; the wide resource share treats crude, refined, and gas as members of the same category.

Azerbaijan: oil and gas infrastructure

Caspian extraction sites and the six export pipelines connecting Azerbaijani fields to Black Sea, Mediterranean and Adriatic terminals.

Finding

Azerbaijan is the case where the wide resource share under-counts the relevant structural change. The regression coefficient predicts roughly no ECI movement from the stable wide share; observed ECI rose by +0.47. The likely mechanism is within-HS27 movement toward refining and gas, which adds complexity inside the broader hydrocarbon category. A finer breakdown of HS27 (the post-2019 sub-code interactions test something similar in spirit) would identify this where the aggregate share cannot.

Chile

Chile is the highest-ECI country in the resource-rich subsample, sitting at −0.14 in 2023 against a cluster median of −0.22 for base-metals exporters. Its wide resource share is 67% in 2023, lower than the petrostates and dominated by base metals (copper at 22%) and ores (copper and iron concentrates at 30%). ECI declined modestly from +0.10 in 1995 to −0.14 in 2023, a slow erosion rather than a structural lock-in. Chile illustrates the ceiling outcome for a resource-rich economy: relatively high ECI within its cluster, but the within-country trend is the wrong direction.

Chile: copper, lithium and the mineral supply chain

Production sites, processing plants and Pacific export ports across the northern mining corridor; switch to the Exports view to trace shipments from each port to 70 destination markets.

Finding

Chile’s wide resource share rose by roughly 20 percentage points across the panel window. The regression coefficient predicts an ECI drag of around −0.09 from this channel; the observed decline was −0.24. The smaller gap between predicted and observed (compared with Congo) is consistent with Chile’s stronger non-resource fundamentals (manufacturing share, institutional quality), which the regression and ML both pick up as positive predictors.

Synthesis

Conclusions

The three pipelines (clustering, panel regression, machine learning) point in the same direction. A country whose export basket shifts further toward natural resources sees its Economic Complexity Index fall by 0.43 points per full-range increase in the wide resource share, or roughly 0.04 points per ten-percentage-point rise, significant at the 1% level under two-way fixed effects and significant at the same level under three additional specifications. Under pooled OLS without country and year fixed effects the coefficient collapses to −0.08 and loses significance, confirming that the within-country signal does the identification work, not rich-versus-poor cross-sectional differences. Across the regularised linear models and XGBoost, the largest positive predictors of ECI are human capital and manufacturing share; both discriminate strongly between countries but do not move enough within a 29-year panel to be identified as within-country drivers.

The four cluster archetypes (mixed extractives, hydrocarbon petrostates, base-metals exporters, precious metals and stones) partition the 64 resource-reliant economies into structurally meaningful groups, and cluster transitions track real production events rather than commodity price cycles: Guyana’s Stabroek oil ramp, Yemen’s wartime collapse of hydrocarbon exports, Ghana’s diversification onto its long-standing gold base, and Mozambique’s gradual move toward floating LNG. Three post-2019 commodity-group interactions test for COVID-period shifts; none clears 10% significance under the headline specification, and LASSO under temporal cross-validation shrinks all three to zero. The COVID-period commodity data may be worth tracking with future panel updates, but the headline finding does not depend on it.

The case studies show how the same coefficient plays out in practice. Republic of Congo is the lock-in pattern: high and rising resource share, ECI deteriorating from −0.74 in 1995 to −1.59 in 2023, no visible diversification in the trade data. Azerbaijan shows where the wide resource share is silent on the relevant structural change: the aggregate share has stayed at 93 to 95% throughout the panel, but the composition shifted from crude oil toward refined products and gas, and ECI improved from an estimated −0.5 in 1995 to −0.52 in 2023. Chile is the ceiling outcome: the highest ECI in the resource-rich subsample, with manufacturing share and institutional quality partly offsetting the resource drag.

Robustness

Sample Sensitivity

The headline finding is checked against four alternatives: a stricter standard-error clustering (M2: two-way), a parsimonious specification with resource × investment interaction terms (M3), a falsification under pooled OLS without fixed effects (M4), and a re-estimation on the observed sample without MICE imputation (M5). The full coefficient table is reported below.

Findings That Survive

The negative coefficient on wide resource share survives across all four fixed-effects specifications (M1, M2, M3, M5), with point estimates between −0.43 and −0.57, all significant at the 1% level. Under pooled OLS without country and year fixed effects (M4), the coefficient collapses to −0.08 and loses significance: the within-country signal is doing the identification, not cross-sectional rich-versus-poor differences.

The MICE imputation does not change the substantive reading. M5, estimated on observations with no imputed values, returns −0.48 (close to the MICE-pooled −0.43). MICE-pooled inference is the more conservative choice on standard errors, but neither the sign nor the magnitude of the headline coefficient depends on the imputation step.

Resource × investment interaction terms are not statistically robust. In M3 the log HCI × wide share interaction is negative and significant at 5% (−1.16), but it reverses the prediction from Mehlum, Moene and Torvik (2006) and loses significance once the wider control set is restored in M1. The log GFCF × wide share interaction is not significant in any specification.

The post-2019 COVID interactions are insignificant in all four fixed-effects specifications. LASSO under temporal cross-validation shrinks them to zero. There is no robust COVID-period signal in this data.

SHAP feature rankings are stable across the M = 5 imputations. The top five features (log Human Capital Index, Manufacturing share, Agriculture share, log Population, wide resource share) carry cross-imputation relative standard deviations below 0.02. The headline ranking does not depend on a particular imputation draw.

Known Limitations

What the analysis does not resolve

Causal direction. The fixed-effects specification identifies within-country correlation between resource share and ECI, not causation. The plausible reverse channel (countries with weakening industrial bases shift back toward extractive exports) cannot be ruled out by panel regression alone.

Cluster heterogeneity. The k = 4 partition is interpretable but conceals within-cluster variation. The hydrocarbon petrostates cluster includes Saudi Arabia and Yemen; the precious cluster includes Botswana and Central African Republic. A k = 5 partition (tested; not adopted) splits petrostates into a crude-only and a mixed-hydrocarbon subgroup, which adds interpretive complexity without changing the regression results.

Imputation for early years. The 1995 to 2001 Comtrade coverage is thinner than later years, and MICE imputation carries more weight in those years. The temporal cross-validation in the ML pipeline does not evaluate predictions before 2003 (the minimum training window is eight years), so the early-period imputations matter less for ML inference than for regression inference.

COVID interactions. The post-2019 indicator captures anything that changed after 2019, not just COVID. Energy market restructuring, the Russia-Ukraine war (from 2022), and the associated commodity price spikes all load on this dummy. The three commodity-group interactions (hydrocarbons, ores, base metals) are all insignificant under M1 in our data. Isolating a clean COVID channel would require a more structured event-study design.

Appendix

Methodology and Supplementary Material

Concepts and Definitions

Economic Complexity Index (ECI). A country-level score built from the structure of its export basket. Countries that export many distinct products, where those products are themselves exported by few other countries, score high. Japan and Switzerland sit at the top of the global ranking; most oil exporters and low-income agricultural economies sit near the bottom. The version used here is the HS92-based ECI from the Atlas of Economic Complexity, recomputed annually as new trade data become available.

Harmonized System (HS) classification. An international customs taxonomy used to classify every traded good. The first two digits identify the chapter (HS27 = mineral fuels, mineral oils and products of their distillation; HS26 = ores, slag and ash; HS71 = natural or cultured pearls, precious or semi-precious stones, precious metals). The next two digits identify the heading (HS2709 = crude petroleum; HS2710 = refined petroleum). This analysis pulls Comtrade data at the four-digit sub-code level within HS27 and at the chapter level for HS26 and HS71.

Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE). A method for handling missing data that draws plausible values for each missing cell from a model fit to the observed data, then re-fits the imputation model with the new values, iterating to convergence. Running the procedure M times (here M = 5) produces M completed datasets; estimates are computed on each and pooled using Rubin’s rules, which account for both within-imputation and across-imputation variance. The country-demeaning option here forces each imputation to preserve country-specific variation rather than borrow across the cross-section.

Resource-Reliance (RR) clustering. The pipeline here takes seven trade shares (coal, crude oil, refined oil, gas, ores, base metals, precious), applies log1p to compress the heavy right tails, reduces to two principal components, and runs K-means with k = 4. The log1p step is what makes the partition robust to price-level shifts: a doubling in oil prices roughly doubles HS27 share for petrostates, and the log transformation compresses that doubling into a small additive shift in the clustering feature.

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations). A method originating in cooperative game theory and adapted to feature importance for machine learning models. Each prediction is decomposed into contributions from each feature, summing to the difference between the model’s prediction and the global mean. SHAP values are stable under feature permutation in a way that raw feature-importance scores from tree models are not, which is why they are preferred for cross-model comparison.

Panel fixed effects. Country fixed effects absorb every time-invariant country characteristic (geography, colonial history, language, founding institutions). Year fixed effects absorb every global shock (commodity price swings, financial crises, the COVID pandemic), to the extent those shocks affect all countries in the same year. What remains is within-country year-over-year variation. The negative wide_resource_share coefficient under FE is identified from countries whose resource share rose or fell from one year to the next, not from the level difference between Saudi Arabia and South Korea.

Methodology and Estimation

Data Pipeline

UN Comtrade pulls at the four-digit HS sub-code level (customsCode = C00, motCode = 0; uniform six-year batches with defensive client-side filtering). FRED, IMF WEO 2025 release, Penn World Tables 11.0, Atlas of Economic Complexity HS92 ECI for the non-trade variables. Country codes harmonised to ISO3 throughout. Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE) with M = 5 imputations and country-demeaning fills macroeconomic gaps. Cache hashing keys on (country, period, HS_code) so re-pulls touch only the missing slices.

Statistical Methods

Panel regression: two-way fixed effects (country and year) with country-clustered standard errors. The main table reports M1 (2WFE) and M4 (pooled OLS); three additional specifications (M2 with two-way clustered SEs, M3 with resource × investment interactions and reduced controls, M5 on the observed-only sample) are reported in the regression appendix. Three post-2019 commodity-group interaction terms test for COVID-period shifts. ML pipeline: LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net, Random Forest, and XGBoost on the structural feature set, with SHAP feature importance. Cluster analysis: log1p-PCA-KMeans on seven trade-share features with k = 4 selected by silhouette. All downstream estimates pooled across the M = 5 imputations.

Full code and data pipeline available on GitHub (notebooks: e1_data_pull, e1b_trade_data, e2_data_prep, e3_clusters, e4_ml, e5_regressions).

Data Sources and Sample Construction

Sample definition

The headline sample for regression and ML is the set of 73 developing economies that have continuous Comtrade reporting from 1995 to 2024 under the HS92 classification, with macroeconomic series available from FRED, IMF WEO, and Penn World Tables 11.0. High-income economies in 1995 are excluded, with the six Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Oman) force-included because their export baskets are clearly resource-dominated despite their per-capita incomes. No rent-share threshold is applied at the sample-construction stage.

The clustering exercise uses a tighter 64-country resource-reliant subsample defined by the mean wide_resource_share exceeding 0.40 across the panel. Countries below the threshold (Bangladesh, India, China, Korea, Turkey, many smaller economies) are kept in the regression and ML panels but are not assigned to a resource cluster.

Principal Component Analysis

The clustering is fit on log-transformed shares of seven commodity sub-codes (coal, crude oil, refined oil, gas, ores, base metals, precious metals), reduced via PCA to two components before K-means. The scatter below shows each resource-rich country positioned in the PC1–PC2 plane, coloured by its assigned cluster. The two loading charts show which commodity shares define each axis.

PCA scatter, coloured by cluster

Each point is one resource-rich country plotted on the first two principal components of its panel-mean export basket. Cluster membership maps onto distinct PC1 positions for petrostates and a separate PC2 spread for precious-metals and base-metals exporters.

PC1 loadings

PC1 separates hydrocarbon-dominated economies (high on crude oil and gas) from those whose baskets lean on solid commodities (ores, base metals, precious).
