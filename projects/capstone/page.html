Beyond the Resource Curse

Capstone with Moody's Ratings on industrial upgrading in resource-rich emerging markets. The work tests which structural conditions allow commodity exporters to develop value-added industries, and identifies three pillars (financial depth, energy infrastructure, and human capital) that consistently predict sustained diversification. The analysis combines clustering, fixed-effects panel regression, and machine learning across a panel of 56 developing countries observed from 1995 to 2019. The sample is selected on three criteria: forest-rent-adjusted natural resource rents above 1% of GDP, not classified as high-income by the World Bank in 1995, and a force-include for the six Gulf states. Case studies of the Republic of Congo, Azerbaijan, and Chile illustrate how the binding constraint shifts with country context.

Motivation

Concepts & Research Question

Economic complexity measures how sophisticated and diverse a country’s exports are. Countries that produce many different, hard-to-make products (pharmaceuticals, machinery, electronics) score high on the Economic Complexity Index (ECI). Countries whose exports are dominated by raw materials score low. Japan, Germany, and Switzerland rank highest; many oil exporters and low-income countries rank lowest.

The resource curse is the observation that countries rich in natural resources often grow more slowly, have weaker institutions, and develop less diverse economies than resource-poor countries. Nigeria and Venezuela are classic examples: abundant natural wealth, low economic complexity. Norway, Canada, and Australia show that resource wealth and economic sophistication can coexist.

The empirical literature finds weak evidence for the curse as a deterministic mechanism. Mehlum, Moene & Torvik (2006) show that resources impede growth only where institutions are “grabber-friendly”; where rule of law is strong, resources accelerate growth. Venables (2016) argues that resource-rich governments tend to convert extraction rents into consumption rather than productive investment. The central question has shifted from whether resources harm growth to under what conditions they do.

Human capital, measured by the World Bank’s Human Capital Index (HCI), captures education and health outcomes in a country’s workforce. It is one of the strongest predictors of economic complexity, but this project tests whether its effect is weakened in countries where natural resources dominate the economy.

Research Question

Do natural resource endowments constrain or enable economic diversification? How do resource-rich countries differ in their paths toward complexity?

The analysis tests whether the resource curse operates uniformly or whether its severity depends on the type of resource, the strength of institutions, and whether human capital investment can overcome the structural disadvantages of resource dependence.

Design

Analytical Approach

The project combines two quantitative strategies with three qualitative case studies. Each strategy answers a different question, and the case studies illustrate how the binding constraint varies by country context.

Unsupervised Learning

Principal Component Analysis reduces the data to its main dimensions. K-means clustering assigns each country to one of six groups based on its resource and development profile, providing structural peer groups for comparison.

Panel Econometrics

Fixed-effects panel regression estimates within-country relationships between resource production and economic complexity. Country and year fixed effects absorb unobserved heterogeneity; standard errors are clustered at the country level.

Machine Learning

Seven algorithms (regularised linear models and tree-based ensembles) test how well resource and institutional variables predict complexity out of sample. SHAP decomposes predictions to identify which variables carry the most weight.

Production data for 20+ commodities are aggregated into four categories (oil, natural gas, coal, and metals) and normalized by GDP to measure resource intensity: how much of a country’s economy depends on extraction, rather than how much it produces in absolute terms.

Results Preview

Three Pillars Behind Industrial Upgrading

Across both the econometric and machine learning analyses, three structural variables consistently predict sustained diversification and the development of value-added industries.

Financial Depth

Domestic Credit to Private Sector. Where firms cannot borrow, resource rents stay in the extractive sector rather than financing new industries. Domestic credit emerges as the single strongest predictor in the ML models.

Energy Infrastructure

Access to Electricity. Manufacturing requires reliable electricity. Countries with low electrification cannot sustain industrial production outside extraction. Electricity access is a consistent predictor across all ML specifications.

Human Capital

Education & Skills (HCI). Without educated workers, countries remain locked into the lowest segments of the value chain. Human capital is positive and significant across all econometric specifications.

Data

Panel Construction

The dataset spans 1995 to 2019 and draws on six primary sources: World Bank World Development Indicators, IMF World Economic Outlook and Capital Stock datasets, V-Dem governance indicators, Penn World Table 10.01, CEPII GeoDist, and commodity production data from the Energy Institute, USGS, and Our World in Data. The cluster analysis (Part II) uses cross-sectional data on the full set of countries with valid production data. The fixed-effects panel regression (Part III) and the ML pipeline (Part IV) use a balanced panel of 56 resource-rich developing countries with reasonably complete coverage of the regression covariates.

Missing values are filled by linear interpolation (capped at three years) followed by K-Nearest Neighbours imputation (k=5, distance-weighted). 20+ commodities are aggregated into four categories (oil, natural gas, coal, metals) and converted to dollar values using a consolidated price file.

A note on sample definition

The sample is selected on three criteria applied to the 1995 snapshot. First, countries with extractive resource rents (oil, gas, coal, and minerals) above 1% of GDP are retained. The World Bank’s total natural resource rents indicator also includes forest rents, which inflates dependence scores for several forestry-driven economies; forest rents are subtracted before the threshold is applied. Second, countries classified as high-income by the World Bank in 1995 (Australia, Brunei, Canada, Norway, and others) are dropped, which restricts the sample to developing economies even where extractive rents are large. Third, the six Gulf states (Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, United Arab Emirates) are force-included regardless of either criterion; several were high-income in 1995, and their 1995 rent shares understate the oil sectors that come online later in the panel. The Robustness section re-estimates the headline specification on three alternative sample definitions (including the uncorrected total-rents construction) to confirm that the main results do not depend on these choices.

Part II

Country Clustering

Not all resource-rich countries are alike. Some are petrostates with minimal manufacturing; others are mining economies with growing industrial bases; some produce a wide spread of resources at moderate scale. To make sense of this variety, countries are grouped by similarity across three subsoil rent shares (oil, gas, mineral, all as % of GDP) and log production per capita.

The grouping uses K-means clustering with k=5, fit on country-level means across the 1995-2019 panel so cluster assignments reflect each country’s structural resource profile rather than a single year’s outcome. Forestry rents are deliberately excluded from the clustering features, because the sample already subtracts forest rents at selection; including them again would reintroduce the same distortion. Cluster names are derived from each centroid, not hard-coded. The technical details of dimensionality reduction and cluster diagnostics are in the appendix.

Diversified Producers (27)

Examples: Argentina, China, India, Indonesia, Malaysia, Mexico, South Africa.

The largest cluster, covering countries with modest resource intensity across multiple categories. Most have growing manufacturing sectors and middle-income status. Resources contribute meaningfully to GDP but are not the dominant growth engine.

Oil Exporters (15)

Examples: Angola, Iran, Iraq, Kazakhstan, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Venezuela.

Oil and gas dominate exports and government revenue, with hydrocarbon rents typically 20-40% of GDP. Wide variation within the cluster: the Gulf states combine high oil dependence with stronger institutions and human capital; Angola, Venezuela, and Yemen show the classic resource-curse profile.

Petrostates (6)

Members: Algeria, Bahrain, Qatar, Russia, Trinidad and Tobago, Uzbekistan.

Highest hydrocarbon production per capita in the sample. Extraction generates exceptionally large per-capita value, allowing rents to substitute for broader productive capacity in the short run. Notable within-cluster heterogeneity: Qatar and Bahrain are wealthy small economies; Russia is a continental producer; Uzbekistan and Trinidad and Tobago are mid-income.

Mining Exporters (5)

Members: Chile, Guinea, Mongolia, Papua New Guinea, Peru.

Metals and minerals dominate the export basket. Mid-range economic complexity and human capital, with downstream processing and manufacturing linkages that vary substantially. Chile and Peru have stronger institutions; Mongolia and Guinea remain more constrained.

Mixed Producers (3)

Members: Dominican Republic, Liberia, Mauritania.

Small residual cluster of low-intensity, low-production economies that do not fit cleanly into any dominant-resource category. Iron ore, nickel, and gold appear in different proportions, but no single resource defines the group.

Part III

Econometric Results

The econometric strategy tests Venables’ (2016) hypothesis that resource-rich countries underperform because they convert extraction rents into consumption rather than productive investment. Following Mehlum, Moene & Torvik (2006), the specification interacts resource production with the two main investment channels (human capital and physical capital formation) rather than estimating a direct resource effect. Country and year fixed effects absorb time-invariant country characteristics and global commodity-price shocks; standard errors are clustered at the country level. Variables entering as interactions are mean-centred (grand mean) before multiplication to reduce collinearity.

Core Specification

$$\begin{aligned} ECI_{it} = \;\beta_0 &+ \beta_1 \text{HCI}_{it} + \beta_2 \text{GFCF}_{it} + \beta_3 \text{Prod}_{it} + \beta_4 \text{Forest}_{it} + \boldsymbol{\gamma}' \mathbf{X}_{it} \\ &+ \beta_8 (\text{HCI}_{it} \times \text{Prod}_{it}) + \beta_9 (\text{GFCF}_{it} \times \text{Prod}_{it}) \\ &+ \beta_{10} (\text{HCI}_{it} \times \text{Forest}_{it}) + \beta_{11} (\text{GFCF}_{it} \times \text{Forest}_{it}) \\ &+ \beta_{12} ECI_{i,t-1} + \epsilon_{it} \end{aligned}$$

where \(\mathbf{X}_{it}\) = Political Stability, Rule of Law, Trade Openness.

Variable Groups

Natural Resources: forestry rents, subsoil production. Investment: Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF), Human Capital Index (HCI). Institutions: rule of law, political stability. Economic: trade openness, lagged ECI.

Identification Strategy

The Forest variable is included separately from subsoil resource production because forest rents in the World Bank Adjusted Net Savings indicator behave differently from extractive rents (point-source vs diffuse, per Isham et al. 2005). The interaction terms test whether the marginal return to investment in human or physical capital is higher in resource-intensive settings.

Key Findings

Human Capital (HCI) is positive and significant in the no-lag specification (Model 3a). With lagged ECI included (Model 3b), past complexity absorbs most of the explanatory variance and the HCI coefficient shrinks toward zero, reflecting how slowly HCI changes within countries. Gross Fixed Capital Formation is positive and significant in both specifications. Production Value carries a small negative coefficient but is generally not statistically distinguishable from zero on its own, consistent with the conditional curse view: resources are not inherently harmful, but their effect depends on how rents are channelled. Institutional controls (rule of law, political stability) lose significance once lagged ECI is included, suggesting their effect operates through long-run structural channels rather than year-on-year variation. None of the four interaction terms reach conventional significance; the chart in the Robustness section shows the descriptive HCI × Production relationship for context.

Transition

Why Machine Learning after Econometrics?

The Problem

Econometric models select variables guided by theory. This ensures interpretability but risks omitting predictors that matter empirically. With 48 available variables across nine thematic groups, a theory-driven specification can only test a small subset; important factors may be missed simply because they fall outside the theoretical framework.

The Solution

All available variables are fed into the ML pipeline, which identifies which carry the most explanatory power. Regularisation (LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net) penalises weak coefficients, shrinking them toward zero. Random Forest calculates feature importance by capturing nonlinear relationships. Where ML and econometrics converge, confidence in those variables is strengthened; where they diverge, ML flags factors the theory-driven model may have overlooked.

Part IV

Machine Learning Insights

Convergence across methods: Human Capital and the HCI × Production interaction rank as top predictors in both the econometric and ML approaches.

Divergence across methods: Domestic credit to the private sector and access to electricity are the strongest ML predictors of ECI, and both are absent from the theory-driven regression specification. This divergence is the central empirical contribution of the ML pipeline: it identifies two structural variables that the Venables-style investment framework does not test directly, but that recur across LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net, and Random Forest.

Part V

Future Risk and Opportunities

Based on current predictor levels, the ensemble model projects which countries are positioned for ECI gains and which are positioned for losses through 2030. Forecasts assume that each country’s structural variables continue along their last-five-year linear trend; predictions are generated iteratively, with each year’s lagged ECI equal to the prior year’s prediction. These projections carry substantial uncertainty over an eleven-year horizon.

Projected ECI Trajectories (2020–2030)

Improving

Chile: macro stability and institutional strength, with green energy abundance providing a base for green industrialisation. Mongolia: heavy investment in education and financial infrastructure alongside mining-linked industrialisation. Equatorial Guinea: very low complexity base, with planned forestry processing likely to deliver ECI gains.

Declining

Malaysia: Fitch downgrade from A− to BBB+ reflects lower institutional quality and political tensions. Saudi Arabia: disappointing results from large projects like NEOM, with private credit heavily concentrated in real estate rather than productive sectors. Kazakhstan: persistent petroleum dependence has worsened, and lagging human capital continues to erode possible complexity gains.

Republic of Congo

Oil accounts for roughly 40% of GDP, 80% of exports, and 60% of government revenues. Only 50% of the population has access to electricity and 13% of roads are paved. 94% of active firms are informal, cutting them off from formal credit and upskilling. Only 18% of adults hold a formal bank account. Education quality is very low: only 60% of children completing primary school progress to secondary, and the country has around 23,000 teachers against an estimated need of 48,000.

Policy Recommendations

Education investment. Delink education spending from oil revenue cycles through a dedicated fund mechanism within the CEMAC framework. Shift focus from broad-based low-capacity schooling to continuous student progression and quality.

Open the electricity sector to private participation. The 2003 Electricity Code already provides the institutional framework; political will to enforce it is needed.

Strengthen oil revenue transparency through independent verification systems and production reporting.

Azerbaijan

Over 45% of what the government classifies as “non-oil” manufacturing is petroleum-related. Infrastructure spending is concentrated on pipelines. SOFAZ (the sovereign wealth fund) transfers to the budget have at times exceeded the fund’s own investment returns. Politically connected contracts, opaque procurement, and SOFAZ’s lack of independence from presidential orders enable rent capture. The State Statistical Committee does not publish a Gini coefficient. Non-performing loans reached 21% after the 2015–2016 banking crisis.

Policy Recommendations

Mitigate rent capture. Establish independent oversight bodies for sovereign wealth fund governance, separating SOFAZ disbursement decisions from presidential authority.

Redirect capital toward adjacent sectors. Prioritise investment in sectors closely related to the existing export basket (petrochemical processing, renewable energy engineering) rather than reinforcing extractive capacity.

Establish independent data institutions. An autonomous national statistics agency, with consistent publication of non-oil deficit projections and SOFAZ sustainability assessments.

Chile

Chile has the strongest institutions in Latin America, yet its ECI has declined since the mid-2000s. R&D spending at 0.34% of GDP is one-sixth of the OECD average. PISA maths scores are 60 points below the OECD average, and worker productivity is 50% below the OECD average. Chilean exports sit in the periphery of the product space, meaning they connect to few other, more complex products. 54% of copper exports go to China for downstream processing rather than being refined domestically. With infrastructure and credit access at near-OECD levels, the binding constraint is human capital quality and the absence of an R&D ecosystem to anchor downstream processing.

Policy Recommendations

Direct lithium revenues toward education quality and applied research, allocating a defined share of rents for technical training institutes and applied research, following Norway’s SINTEF model.

Build the domestic mining supply chain through procurement preferences for domestic suppliers and dedicated credit lines with risk-sharing facilities.

Invest in logistics infrastructure along the northern mining corridor: port expansion, rail gauge standardisation, and energy connections to support proposed lithium processing facilities and reduce freight costs.

Synthesis

Conclusions and Implications

No single policy lever is sufficient across all settings. The relative importance of financial depth, infrastructure, and human capital shifts with institutional and macroeconomic conditions. The three pillars provide a framework for identifying which constraint is binding in each country context.

01

Financial Depth

Domestic credit to the private sector emerged as the single strongest ML predictor. In Congo, only a minority of firms have access to financing, which increases barriers to firm formalisation. Expanding bank financing is a precondition for resource revenues to reach the productive economy.

02

Infrastructure & Governance

Electricity access is a consistent predictor across all ML models, but infrastructure spending only translates into diversification when governance channels it beyond the resource sector. In Azerbaijan, redirecting investment toward petrochemical processing and renewable energy engineering would target sectors adjacent to the existing export basket.

03

Human Capital

Positive and significant across all econometric specifications. Chile has the strongest institutions in Latin America, yet its ECI has declined since the mid-2000s. Directing lithium revenues toward technical training and applied research, following Norway’s SINTEF model, would build the workforce needed for downstream processing.

04

Different Starting Points Need Different Strategies

The five-cluster typology and the three case studies confirm that the binding constraint shifts as countries develop. Congo faces failures in all three pillars simultaneously; Azerbaijan faces a governance and Dutch Disease problem; Chile has the institutions and infrastructure but lacks the human capital quality and R&D base to move into downstream processing. Structural change requires sustained, long-horizon interventions.

Robustness

Sample Sensitivity

The 56-country headline sample is constructed by subtracting forest rents from the World Bank’s total natural resource rents indicator, applying a 1%-of-GDP threshold, dropping 1995 high-income economies, and force-including the six Gulf states. To confirm that the main findings do not depend on this construction, the headline specification is re-estimated on three alternative sample definitions, including the uncorrected total-rents subsample (which adds forestry-driven economies) and intermediate variants. HAC-panel standard errors (Bartlett kernel, 3 lags) are also reported as a more conservative inference alternative.

Findings That Survive

The negative coefficient on resource production survives across all four sample definitions. The point estimate moves modestly between samples but remains negative.

The positive GFCF × Production interaction also survives, with similar magnitude across samples. Investment channels remain the most consistent moderator of the resource-production association.

Human capital’s coefficient is more sensitive to sample composition than to specification choice. Its magnitude shrinks once electricity access enters the specification, consistent with the indirect-channel reading: HCI’s effect operates partly through infrastructure rather than directly.

Methodological Notes

Data Processing

20+ commodities aggregated into four categories (oil, natural gas, coal, metals). Production values computed as physical quantity × market price (USD). All features standardised (mean=0, SD=1) before PCA, clustering, and regularised regression. Missing values handled by linear interpolation (capped at three years) followed by KNN imputation (k=5, distance-weighted). The 56-country headline sample is constructed by subtracting forest rents from World Bank Adjusted Net Savings, applying a 1%-of-GDP threshold, dropping 1995 high-income economies, and force-including the six Gulf states. The Robustness section reports estimates on three alternative sample definitions.

Statistical Methods

PCA: 2 components, 50.6% variance explained (PC1 28.6%, PC2 22.0%). K-means: k=5 on country panel means across 1995-2019, using oil, gas, and mineral rent shares plus log production per capita. Panel regression: two-way fixed effects (country and year) with country-clustered standard errors. The headline specification includes lagged ECI and four interaction terms (HCI × Prod, GFCF × Prod, HCI × Forest, GFCF × Forest). ML pipeline: regularised regression (LASSO, Ridge, Elastic Net) and Random Forest, with cross-validation and a temporal train/test split. Bootstrap inference: 200 country-block resamples.

Full code and data pipeline available on GitHub or on request.

Supplementary Data

Resource Diversity & Intensity Indices

Diversity uses Shannon entropy to measure how many different types of resources a country produces at a globally significant level. It is highest when production is spread evenly across categories (fossil fuels, base metals, precious metals, battery/strategic metals, industrial minerals) and lowest when concentrated in just one. Only resources where the country holds at least 0.5% of global production are counted. A power transformation (x1.5) rewards market dominance: holding 20% of global cobalt production counts for more than holding 2% of global coal.

Intensity measures total resource production value as a share of GDP. Values range from near-zero for service economies to above 15% for Kuwait and Congo Republic.
