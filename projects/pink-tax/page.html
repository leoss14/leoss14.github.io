Retail pricing analysis

Gendered Pricing and Inflation in UK Retail

Two complementary views of the same question: do female-coded and male-coded personal-care products differ in price, and did the 2022-2024 inflation wave move them differently? A cross-sectional analysis of 12,832 products from three UK supermarkets, paired with chain-linked ONS item indices for 28 personal-care items between September 2019 and December 2024.

Summary

What this project finds

"Pink tax" claims usually take one of two forms: that female-coded products cost more than male-coded equivalents at any given moment, or that prices for female-coded products have risen faster during inflationary episodes. This project tests both. The first uses 12,832 grocery products scraped from three UK supermarkets, classified into female, male and neutral categories using text, color and metadata features, then run through a sequence of regressions. The second uses ONS item-level price indices for 28 personal-care items, chain-linked across annual rebases and aggregated into gendered baskets.

Both halves point to the same answer. Within stores, female products are cheaper on average than male products in raw terms (a -9% gap), and the gap becomes statistically indistinguishable from zero once product category is controlled for. Over time, the female personal-care basket grew slower than the male basket between January 2022 and December 2024 (+5.5% vs +9.3%), and the difference is part of a multi-year trend rather than a wave-specific divergence. In both views, category composition drives the headline number, not within-category pricing discrimination.

Context

Background

The "pink tax" hypothesis claims that products marketed to women cost more than otherwise similar products marketed to men. The strongest version is within-category discrimination: a women's razor priced higher than a men's razor of comparable specification. A weaker version is composition-driven: women buy a different mix of products on average, so their typical basket costs more even if no individual category is mispriced. The two have very different implications. Within-category discrimination would warrant scrutiny of pricing practices and possibly intervention; composition reflects consumer preferences and product differentiation, and looks more like a marketing phenomenon than a regulatory problem.

The academic literature has generally found weaker evidence for the strong version than tabloid headlines suggest. Moshary, Tuchman and Bhatia (2023), using US scanner data, find that adjusting for product attributes largely closes the headline pink tax gap. This project applies a similar empirical strategy to UK supermarket data, then extends the question to a time-series dimension: even if levels are not systematically higher for female products at a moment in time, do their prices move differently through an inflationary episode?

Two scales, one question

The two halves of the project work at different scales. Part I looks at the macro view: monthly ONS item-level price indices for personal-care products between September 2019 and December 2024, aggregated into gendered baskets and tracked over time. This captures national average price movements across the inflation wave. Part II looks at the micro view: a snapshot of 12,832 specific products listed at three UK supermarkets, with machine-learning gender classification and regression-based pink tax estimation. Together they let the same hypothesis be tested across two independent data sources.

Part I

Over time: gendered personal-care inflation, 2019 to 2024

The UK saw its sharpest inflation wave in four decades between early 2022 and mid-2023. Headline CPI rose roughly 18% between January 2022 and December 2024, driven by energy, food and services. The question here is whether female-coded and male-coded personal-care items participated in that wave evenly. The unit of analysis is the individual ONS item index: one number per item per month, aggregated by ONS from many underlying price quotes and meant to reflect average national price movement for that item.

Data

Sample window: September 2019 to December 2024, 64 monthly observations per item. The starting month is set by data availability: ONS published aggregated monthly item-index files from September 2019 onward; earlier months are bundled into annual files. The 28 items in scope cover the personal-care categories most often discussed in pink tax debates, coded as female (9 items including women's haircuts, women's hair colourant, foundation, mascara, lip gloss, nail varnish, women's highlighting, manicure, and period products), male (3 items: men's haircuts, razor cartridge blades, electric razors), and neutral (15 items including toothpaste, shampoo, deodorant, moisturiser, conditioner, perfume, body lotion and toilet rolls). Item IDs and gender codings are stable across the sample window with one exception: the period-products code switched from tampons (ID 520206) to sanitary towels (ID 520256) in February 2023. The two series are bridged through a level-shift rescale at the changeover month.

Annual rebasing and chain-linking

ONS item indices rebase to January = 100 each calendar year. Within a year, the published index for month m in year y is the price ratio against January of year y, multiplied by 100. The January index of each year is itself a chain-link value: the ratio of that January's price to the previous January's. To compare prices across years (say, December 2024 to January 2022), the within-year ratios must be multiplied through the January chain-link values. Without this step, naive year-on-year comparison understates cumulative inflation by an order of magnitude.

What an item index is

An ONS item index is a one-number summary of the average price movement for a single, narrowly defined item (for example, "razor cartridge blades" or "women's haircut") between two dates. It is constructed from many individual price quotes collected each month from a fixed sample of UK retailers. The index value itself carries no price-level information: it is a ratio. Comparing two indices for different items at the same date tells you their relative price growth since the common base, not their relative price levels.

Why this rules out a difference-in-differences design

The analysis was originally set up as a difference-in-differences around January 2022, with female-coded items as the treatment group and neutral items as the control. The chart above documents why that design does not work: the parallel-trends assumption fails. The female series and the neutral series are not on parallel trajectories in the pre-period; the differential is already declining when the inflation wave starts, and the decline continues through the wave without a visible structural break. The post-wave coefficient in a DiD would pick up the average of a smooth trend, not the impact of an event. The analysis is therefore framed as descriptive: it documents what happened, without claiming the inflation wave caused it. The underlying methodological judgement was that flagging a parallel-trends failure openly is preferable to estimating a DiD and ignoring the diagnostic.

Basket-weighted comparison

The within-item analysis above weights each item equally. A more representative summary applies expenditure weights so that items women actually spend more on (haircuts, period products) count for more in the female basket, and similarly for the male basket. The chart below builds a Laspeyres index for each gendered basket: a weighted average of the chain-linked item indices, with weights fixed at the start of the wave. The two baskets follow each other closely through 2022 and most of 2023, then visibly diverge from late 2023 onward.

The female personal-care basket grew by about 5.5% between January 2022 and December 2024, the male basket by about 9.3%. Both grew much less than headline UK CPI (~18% over the same window), which is consistent with personal care being a slow-inflating category. The female-male gap of about 4 percentage points is real but is part of a longer-running pattern; the inflation wave did not create it.

One-paragraph takeaway for Part I

Across the 2019-2024 window, female-coded personal-care items inflated more slowly than male-coded and neutral items. The pattern is monotone and predates January 2022, so the inflation wave neither created the gap nor markedly widened it. By December 2024, the gendered basket Laspeyres indices stood at 105.5 (female) and 109.3 (male), implying a -3.9 pp basket-weighted differential against the typical female consumer, in the opposite direction from the strong "pink tax" claim.

Part II

Across products: three UK supermarkets, snapshot

The second half of the project zooms in: instead of national average price indices, it uses individual product listings scraped from three UK supermarket websites. The aim is to test whether the within-category gap survives controls. The empirical strategy is two-stage: first, classify each product as female, male or neutral using its text and visual features; second, regress log price on the female indicator with progressively richer controls. If the gap is real, it should remain after controlling for product category. If the gap is composition, it should disappear.

Data and gender classification

The dataset contains 21,436 products from Tesco, Morrisons, and ASDA. Categories unlikely to be gendered (food, electronics, pet supplies, cleaning products) were dropped, leaving 12,832 products in personal-care, cosmetics and toiletry-adjacent categories. Training labels came from two sources: regex extraction of explicit gender terms in product descriptions ("for women", "men's", and similar), yielding 1,075 female and 844 male products; and 259 independently human-coded products. Class-balanced training set of 2,532 observations (844 per class), split 75/25 into train and held-out test. 329 features in total: TF-IDF on product descriptions (150 features), TF-IDF on category breadcrumbs (80 features), 93 color features extracted from product images via K-means, and 6 metadata features (store ID, price, unit price).

A stricter test uses the 259 independently human-coded products as ground truth. On that set the best model achieves 70.0% accuracy, a 10 percentage point drop from the keyword-derived test labels. The gap is expected: human coders pick up brand positioning, packaging design and imagery cues that the feature set does not capture. Applied to all 12,832 products, the classifier produces 9,191 neutral (71.6%), 2,257 female (17.6%) and 1,384 male (10.8%) labels, with 533 implicitly female and 278 implicitly male products (predictions above 50% confidence for products with no explicit gender term).

Estimating the within-store pink tax

With gender labels assigned, the second stage estimates the price differential between female- and male-targeted products. All regressions use log-transformed prices as the dependent variable, so coefficients approximate percentage differences (a coefficient of −0.10 corresponds to roughly a 10% price gap). Standard errors are heteroskedasticity-robust (HC1). The strategy is to add controls progressively. If the female coefficient remains negative after controlling for product category, female products are systematically cheaper than male products within comparable categories. If it flips or becomes insignificant, the raw gap reflects composition.

Among the 1,919 explicitly gendered products (1,075 female, 844 male), female products have a lower mean price (£7.03 vs £8.15) and lower median (£4.00 vs £4.50). The raw price gap is already negative, which contradicts the strong pink tax claim before any regression is run. The regressions ask how that −9% raw gap behaves as controls accumulate.

The R² jump from 0.006 to 0.672 when category fixed effects are introduced is the key finding. Product category explains the vast majority of price variation. The raw negative gap arises because female products are concentrated in lower-priced categories (personal care, intimate care) while male products span a wider price range. Within the same category, no statistically significant gap remains.

Quantile regression

OLS estimates the average effect. Quantile regression estimates the gap at different points of the price distribution. Without category controls, the female coefficient is negative across most quantiles (consistent with the OLS raw gap). With category controls, the cheapest quantiles (10th, 25th) show a sizeable positive gap (+43%, +25%; both p < 0.01) while the median and upper quantiles are not distinguishable from zero. Read literally, this looks like a floor-price effect on budget female products. In practice, only 11 of 96 categories contain enough gendered products for within-category comparison, and the number of observations at the tails of these distributions is small. The large positive coefficient at the 10th percentile is a thin-tail estimate and should not be the headline finding; it is reported here as a sensitivity check rather than evidence of systematic floor-price discrimination.

Within-category and three-way comparison

Restricting to the 11 categories with at least 3 female and 3 male products, the median within-category female premium is +7.4% and the weighted mean is +26.2%, though the weighted mean is heavily influenced by an incontinence subcategory with very small male sample. Most individual gaps are not significant under bootstrap 95% confidence intervals. Greeting cards show a modest female premium across sub-categories; shower products show a male premium. A separate three-way comparison using all 12,832 products with neutral as reference finds female products 16.4% cheaper than neutral (p < 0.001), male products 2.9% cheaper than neutral (not significant). This confirms that gendered products as a whole sit in lower price tiers than the broader product mix.

Color predictors

Color information was extracted from product images using K-means clustering on pixel RGB values, with the three dominant colors mapped to a 31-color palette. Coverage was limited: extractions succeeded for 5,619 products, all from one of the three stores (the other two had expired image CDN URLs at the time of scraping). Color-based findings should therefore be read as indicative of marketing conventions visible in the data that survived, not as evidence that those conventions hold uniformly across UK retailers.

Color frequency among explicitly gendered products. Small samples (n ≈ 32 per gender), so frequencies are approximate.

LASSO coefficients from a binary female-male classifier. Positive values predict female; negative predict male.

Patterns are in line with marketing conventions: pinks, lavender and purples predict female; black, brown and navy predict male. Some less obvious results appear (tan has a strong female coefficient, likely from foundation and cosmetics packaging). To quantify whether color adds anything beyond text and metadata, the L1 logistic regression was trained twice, with and without the 93 color features. The improvement was modest: +2.2 percentage points in accuracy at the full-sample level. On the subset where colors are actually observed (the store with intact image URLs), HGB with color features achieves 84.0% accuracy versus 81.9% without (a +2.1 pp delta), suggesting the aggregate-level estimate understates the contribution of color in the covered subsample.

Word predictors

Text features (TF-IDF on product descriptions and on category breadcrumbs) do the most classification work. The HGB model's feature importances pick out the words that most strongly push a product toward each gender class. Brand names ("Lynx" for male) are among the strongest predictors, but the single most influential feature is the category breadcrumb: UK retailers file products under different navigation paths depending on gender even when the underlying products are functionally similar. "Removal" (hair removal) and "intimate" appear in female-coded paths; "toiletries", "razors" and "blades" sit on male-coded paths. The classifier is partly learning the retailers' own taxonomic decisions.

Variation across stores

Splitting the raw-gap regression by store reveals heterogeneity. Three anonymised stores appear in the data (the actual chain names cannot be verified from the scraped breadcrumbs alone, so they are labelled by sample size: Store A is the largest, Store C the smallest). Store A shows a large and significant female discount of 18.6%; the two other stores show no significant raw gap. Once category fixed effects are added to a pooled interaction model, the within-store gap in Store A shrinks to about −4% (no longer significant). Store-level variation in raw gaps is therefore another reflection of composition: different stores stock different mixes of gendered products, which produces different headline numbers even when within-category pricing looks similar.

One-paragraph takeaway for Part II

Across the cross-section of 12,832 supermarket products, the raw female price gap of −9% becomes statistically indistinguishable from zero once product category is controlled for, with R² jumping from 0.006 to 0.672 on the addition of category fixed effects. Unit prices show essentially no gap (+0.7%, p = 0.92). Store-level heterogeneity reflects product-mix differences, not within-category discrimination. The "pink tax" in these data is a composition effect, not a price effect.

Synthesis

Two scales, one conclusion

The two halves of the project are independent in their data sources, time frames and methods. They converge on the same picture.

01

No within-category pink tax in the snapshot

Female products in three UK supermarkets are 9-10% cheaper than male products in raw comparisons. Once product category is controlled for, the gap is statistically indistinguishable from zero and the unit-price differential is essentially zero (+0.7%, p = 0.92). The raw gap is a composition fact, not a pricing fact.

02

No widening through the inflation wave

Across 2022-2024, the female personal-care basket grew by about 5.5% and the male basket by about 9.3% (a -3.9 pp gap, favouring female consumers). The differential is a continuation of a pre-existing trend that started in 2019, not a wave-driven divergence. A planned difference-in-differences design was abandoned because the parallel-trends diagnostic failed.

03

The signal is in product mix

Across both halves, what is gendered is the product mix: retailers segment products by gender through shelf placement, category labelling and brand positioning. Female-coded categories (intimate care, period products, women's haircuts) sit in different price ranges from male-coded ones (razors, men's haircuts), and that is what produces the raw gaps and the basket inflation differential.

04

Implications for the policy conversation

Public debate on the pink tax often assumes within-category discrimination. Both halves of this analysis fail to find it in UK personal-care data. That does not make gendered marketing innocuous; it means that the policy lever is product segmentation and category design, not within-category price regulation. The right intervention depends on which margin one wishes to move.

Limitations

Classification

Products are classified using explicit keyword matching and a trained classifier. Both approaches miss implicitly gendered products: items marketed through color, imagery or brand positioning without explicit terms. The human-coded sample showed that keyword-based labels miss roughly 23% of human-perceived gendered products.

Category granularity

Only 11 product categories had sufficient products of both genders for within-category comparison in the snapshot. Finer-grained categories would improve comparability but reduce sample sizes further. The quantile regression tail estimates rest on particularly small subsamples and should be read accordingly.

Product equivalence

The regression controls for category and description text but does not account for product quality, ingredients, or size beyond what unit price captures. A "women's razor" and a "men's razor" in the same breadcrumb category may differ in blade count, handle design or packaging.

Data coverage

Color features are available only for one of the three stores (5,619 of 12,832 products) because image CDN URLs at the other two had expired by the time of color extraction. Results may also not generalise to other product categories (clothing, electronics) or other markets. The ONS item-index sample is restricted to 28 personal-care items and is not representative of all gendered consumption.

Index aggregation

The Laspeyres baskets in Part I use illustrative single-adult expenditure weights. Reweighting with actual LCFS-derived shares is expected to nudge the magnitude of the basket gap but not its sign or order, because the underlying chain-linked item indices are the same and the gender-coded categories differ in inflation trajectory by more than the weighting differences.

Identification

Part I is descriptive, not causal. A difference-in-differences around January 2022 was tried and abandoned because the female-neutral cumulative price differential has been declining monotonically since at least 2019. Parallel trends fails. A causal estimate of the inflation wave's gendered impact would need a different identification strategy (cross-country variation, instrumental variables, or an item-level shock such as the period products VAT change).

Methodological notes

Cross-sectional pipeline (Part II)

21,436 raw products from three UK supermarket websites. Filtered to 12,832 in gendered-relevant categories. Training labels: regex extraction (1,919 explicit) + human coding (259). Class- balanced training set of 2,532, split 75/25. Five classifiers compared; best is HGB with 80.1% test accuracy and 70.0% agreement on the human-coded gold set. 329 features (150 description TF-IDF, 80 breadcrumb TF-IDF, 93 color, 6 metadata). Regressions: OLS with log(price), HC1 robust SE. Within-category significance via 1,000-draw bootstrap 95% CIs. Quantile regressions at five quantiles.

Time-series pipeline (Part I)

64 monthly ONS item-index CSV files (Sep 2019 to Dec 2024) downloaded with HTTP backoff on rate limits. ITEM_INDEX values chain-linked across annual rebases using the published January link factor. 28 items: 9 female-coded, 3 male-coded, 15 neutral. Period-products bridge across the Feb 2023 item code change (tampons to sanitary towels) via level-shift rescale. Laspeyres and Tornqvist basket indices with illustrative single-adult weights. Diagnostic event study revealed parallel-trends failure; DiD design dropped in favour of descriptive reporting.
