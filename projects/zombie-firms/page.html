Spatial econometrics and panel data

Zombie Firms in Italian Manufacturing

Replication and expansion of a Bocconi undergraduate thesis to all 20 Italian regions, 2016 to 2024. The panel covers 74,726 manufacturing firms over 351,220 firm-years. The standard McGowan-style spillover measure at province by sector produces a null effect on healthy-firm investment. The same firms measured by inverse-distance-weighted zombie density at a 100 km bandwidth produce a significant 2.9 percentage point drop in healthy-firm investment (p = 0.036).

Background

Concepts and research design

A zombie firm is a business that fails to generate enough operating profit to cover its interest expenses but continues to operate because lenders roll over its debt rather than force restructuring. The standard quantitative marker is an interest coverage ratio (ICR) below 1, where ICR is operating profit divided by interest paid. A firm with ICR less than 1 cannot service its debt from its own earnings.

Zombie firms tie up resources (labour, capital, market share, bank credit) that would otherwise flow elsewhere. When banks roll over loans to zombies to avoid booking losses, healthy firms in the same market face higher borrowing costs and invest less. This is the "evergreening" channel from the post-1990s Japanese banking literature (Caballero, Hoshi and Kashyap, 2008), brought to post-2008 Europe by McGowan, Andrews and Millot (2018).

Three definitions used in the literature

Definition choice changes measured prevalence by an order of magnitude:

Label Definition 2022 share
Weak ICR ICR < 1 in the current year 15.4%
McGowan et al. (2018) ICR < 1 for at least 3 consecutive years AND firm age ≥ 10 3.4%
Storz et al. (2017) ICR < 1 AND ROA < 0 AND negative equity, same year 3.2%

McGowan is the primary measure used here. The three-year requirement filters out transitory shocks: weak-ICR jumps to 25.5 percent in COVID 2020, McGowan barely moves (+0.6 points), because firms that recover after one bad year never enter the classification. The age filter excludes young firms running growth-phase deficits.

Research Question
Do zombie firms suppress investment in healthy neighbours, and at what geographic scale is the effect detectable?

Part I documents prevalence across time, sector, and geography. Part II runs the standard McGowan spillover regression at province by sector and gets a null. Part III replaces the administrative unit with inverse-distance weighting at 25, 50, and 100 km radii, where a significant spillover only emerges at the widest bandwidth. Part IV trains an early-warning ML model for zombie status one year ahead.

Data

Sources and sample

Firm-level data comes from Orbis (Bureau van Dijk) under the LSE institutional licence. The search universe is Italian firms with NACE Rev. 2 primary codes 10 to 33 (manufacturing), with at least 10 employees in at least one year between 2016 and 2025, totaling 88,690 firms before regional batch filtering. The export covers ~300 variables per firm including profit and loss, balance sheet, employment, ownership, and NUTS3 location codes, from 2008 to 2024.

Regional batches are merged through a custom pipeline: direct-XML parsing (the openpyxl stylesheet path fails on these files), a year-by-variable column map for the 2011 to 2014 loans block, and BvD-ID deduplication across batches. Geocoding

Postcode-centroid geocoding

Firms are mapped to coordinates via GeoNames IT.txt, which contains 4,735 unique Italian CAP postcodes and their centroids under a CC-BY 4.0 licence. When a CAP contains multiple localities, the mean centroid is used. Part I

Prevalence: time, sector, geography

Under the McGowan definition, the zombie share in Italian manufacturing rose from 2.7 percent in 2018 to a peak of 4.4 percent in 2022 before declining to 3.9 percent in 2024. The 2020 COVID year produces a 25.5 percent spike under the weak-ICR measure but only a 0.6 percentage point rise under McGowan, validating the role of the three-year consecutive requirement in distinguishing persistent distress from transitory shocks.

Zombie share by year under the three definitions. Weak-ICR spikes in COVID; McGowan and Storz barely move, which is what a persistence-based definition is supposed to do.

Sector heterogeneity

Sector shares vary widely. Beverages (10.9 percent), Coke and refined petroleum (7.8 percent), and Pharmaceuticals (7.2 percent) lead in 2022; Fabricated metals, Repair and installation, and Wood products sit at 2.1 to 2.8 percent. The mix at the top (food, energy-intensive, regulated) tracks the post-2021 input-cost squeeze.

Part II

Baseline spillover regressions: a null result

The standard test of the zombie-congestion hypothesis in the recent literature (McGowan, Andrews and Millot 2018; Andrews and Petroulakis 2019) regresses investment or employment outcomes of healthy firms on the share of zombie firms in their immediate market, conventionally defined as the province by 2-digit NACE sector by year cell. The key regressor is constructed as a leave-one-out (LOO) measure to avoid a firm mechanically contributing to its own congestion term:

ZombieShare−ipst = (Npst · ZombieSharepst − Zit) ÷ (Npst − 1)

where the subscripts denote province p, sector s, and year t. All models include firm fixed effects and year fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the province by sector level. Four model variants are estimated:

Model Sample Dependent Coef. (SE) p
M1 Baseline Non-zombie firms Investment rate −0.0032 (0.0092) 0.725
M2 Baseline Non-zombie firms Employment growth −0.0041 (0.0152) 0.788
M3 Credit interaction Non-zombie firms Investment rate −0.0088 (0.0119) 0.460
M4 Own zombie effect All firms Investment rate −0.0029 (0.0011) 0.007

Coefficients with 95 percent CIs. Only M4 (own-zombie) clearly separates from zero.

The spillover effect on investment (M1) and employment growth (M2) is negative across all specifications but never significant. M4 is the one robust baseline finding: a McGowan zombie invests about 0.3 percentage points less in its zombie years than in its non-zombie years (p = 0.007), controlling for size and lagged financial health. The estimate survives every robustness check run on it.

Province-sector zombie congestion against mean healthy-firm investment. The flat binned line tracks the M1 null.

Robustness

The null holds across eight robustness specifications varying the ICR lag structure and the geographic subsample. The direction is always negative, never significant. The unconsolidated-only subsample (U1, U2 firms, 96.9 percent) gives near-identical coefficients, ruling out a consolidation-mix artefact.

The capital-weighted version is borderline

Weighting zombies by their share of province-sector tangible fixed assets instead of headcount gives −0.0054 (SE 0.0030, p = 0.077). Count-share and capital-share correlate at only 0.46, so they measure different things. The SE drops by 3x, pointing to a few large zombies tying up disproportionate capital as the relevant margin. Borderline, not headline.

Credit channel: no evidence

Three tests for the evergreening channel (zombies crowding out credit to healthy neighbours) all come back null at province-sector level. Zombie congestion has no effect on healthy-firm financial intensity (+0.0004, p = 0.59) or on log loan growth (−0.042 nationally, +0.011 in the North). Adding financial intensity as a control attenuates the investment effect by only 6.4 percent. Credit cost is not the mediator at this scale.

Part III

Distance instead of administrative boundaries

Two ways to read the null. Either zombie spillovers do not exist in Italian manufacturing in this period, or the geographic unit is wrong. NUTS3 provinces in Italy have an average radius of about 30 km, so the standard measure averages zombie density over only the nearest 25 to 30 km. If the real spillover operates over larger neighbourhoods, the province aggregate will dilute the signal.

To test this, the congestion term is recomputed using firm-level coordinates rather than administrative cells. For each healthy firm in each year, an inverse-distance-weighted zombie share is calculated over neighbours within radii of 25, 50, and 100 km:

ZombieShareIDWit(b) = Σj∈Nit(b) wij · Zjt

where Nit(b) is the set of firms within bandwidth b of firm i in year t, and the weights wij are inverse-distance with row-normalisation. The resulting measure is then used in the same M1 baseline specification (investment rate of healthy firms on zombie share, with firm and year fixed effects and clustered standard errors).

The bandwidth sweep

Geographic unit Coefficient SE p Verdict

Coefficient on healthy-firm investment, by geographic unit. The effect grows monotonically with bandwidth and hits significance at 100 km.

The effect strengthens monotonically with bandwidth. A 100 km radius captures roughly the size of a labour catchment area or a regional banking relationship, whereas a NUTS3 province captures only the nearest 25 to 30 km. The standard measure has been averaging over the wrong unit.

What the pattern rules out

The monotonic strengthening from 25 to 100 km is informative about the mechanism. A pure bank-branch story would imply distance decay: the coefficient should be strongest at 25 km and fade with bandwidth. The opposite is observed.

Three mechanisms fit the pattern. Regional labour markets in Italian manufacturing span 50 to 100 km, so zombies hoarding skilled workers depress labour mobility over that range. Supply chain networks among Northern manufacturers operate at provincial and inter-provincial scale. Bank relationships, while locally sourced on average, run across regional networks for the medium-sized firms that dominate the panel. Credit rationing at regional scale, which province-level averaging cannot detect, would produce the same shape.

Why the NUTS2 administrative measure also fails

Going one administrative level up from province (NUTS2 by sector) gives a sign-flipped, insignificant +0.0045. The zombie-heavy NUTS2 regions are also the most economically active, so the aggregate confounds zombie density with industrial dynamism. Distance weighting cuts through this by weighting nearby zombies wherever they sit administratively. That the NUTS2 null and the 100 km significant result diverge is itself evidence that administrative boundaries are not the right unit here.

Part IV

Machine learning early-warning model

Can financial ratios at year t predict McGowan zombie status at year t+1? The classifier uses a time-based split (train 2016 to 2021, validation 2022, test 2023) and AUPRC as the primary metric, which is the appropriate measure when class imbalance is heavy (the test-set zombie rate is 3.8 percent).

Feature selection: dropping ICR

The McGowan target is defined as three consecutive years of ICR below 1. Including current ICR or ICR lags as features lets the model rediscover the classification rule rather than forecast deterioration. The model is therefore fit twice: once with ICR features included (the leaky baseline) and once with them dropped (the legitimate spec). The gap between the two AUPRC numbers is the size of the leakage.

Test-set performance

Model With ICR (leaky) Without ICR (clean) Δ AUPRC
Logistic regression 0.298 0.174 −0.12
Random Forest 0.846 0.232 −0.61
XGBoost 0.836 0.304 −0.53
Naive (prevalence) 0.042 0.038 n/a

With ICR features, XGBoost reaches test AUPRC 0.836. Without them, 0.304. The tree models lose more than half a unit of AUPRC; the linear model loses only 0.12. Trees can carve hard splits at the ICR threshold; linear models cannot, so they were never exploiting the leakage to begin with.

The 0.304 figure is what the page reports as the model's actual performance. It is 8x the prevalence baseline of 0.038 and 1.7x the linear-model floor.

Both specs across three model families. The gap between rose (with ICR) and navy (without ICR) is the magnitude of the leakage that the published spec removes.

What the legitimate model picks up

Mean absolute SHAP values for the XGBoost no-ICR spec. The year-on-year change in leverage is the top contributor, followed by log total assets, financial intensity, and leverage lags. The model is reading rising debt and shrinking scale, not the ICR threshold.

Forecast horizon

Test AUPRC and ratio over naive at t+1, t+2, t+3. AUPRC falls from 0.29 (one year) to 0.24 (three years); the ratio over prevalence falls from 7.7x to 5.6x. The signal weakens with horizon but does not collapse.

Synthesis

Conclusions

Four findings, ordered by how confident the evidence is.

01

Distance-weighted spillovers exist where province-based ones do not

The standard province by sector spillover specification produces a null coefficient. The same data measured by inverse-distance-weighted zombie share at a 100 km radius produces a coefficient of −0.0289 on healthy-firm investment, significant at 5 percent. The effect strengthens monotonically with bandwidth from 25 to 100 km, ruling out a hyper-local bank-branch story and pointing instead to regional labour markets, supply chains, or banking networks. The standard literature's null findings on zombie spillovers in Europe may partly reflect mismeasured geography.

02

The own-zombie investment penalty is robust

McGowan zombies invest 0.3 percentage points less in their zombie years than in their non-zombie years (p = 0.007). The estimate is stable across sample restrictions, ICR lag specifications, and consolidation code subsamples. It is the most defensible finding on the page.

03

Capital is more relevant than headcount for congestion

Replacing the firm-count share of zombies with their share of province-sector tangible fixed assets produces a borderline-significant coefficient (p = 0.077). The correlation between count-share and capital-share measures is only 0.46, so they are not interchangeable. Large zombie firms tying up disproportionate industry capital matter more than the number of zombie firms.

04

The credit-cost channel is not detectable here

Three province-sector tests (zombie congestion on healthy-firm financial intensity, on log loan growth, and as a mediator of the investment effect) all come back null. Unobservable credit rationing or sub-province geography remain possible, but the observable financial ratios do not show the channel. If credit drives Finding 1, it does so through a mechanism this data cannot reach.

Limitations

Survivorship in Orbis

The Orbis panel covers firms that exist at the time of extraction. Firms that failed and deregistered are present only insofar as their historical filings remain in the database. This biases the panel toward survivors, particularly in the late 2010s, and probably understates the true historical zombie share. The bias is largest where the zombie process ended in liquidation rather than acquisition.

Geocoding is at postcode centroid

CAP coordinates locate firms at the centroid of their postcode area. Within a postcode, multiple firms collapse to the same point, which affects near-distance neighbour counts. The 25 km bandwidth result is therefore the most affected; the 100 km headline is robust to this issue because the bandwidth far exceeds any postcode radius.

Identification at finer scales

The significant 100 km result rules out the very local bank-branch story but does not separate the labour-market, supply-chain, and regional-banking channels from each other. Establishing the dominant mechanism would require additional data: matched employer-employee records for the labour channel, input-output tables for supply chains, or branch-level bank balance sheets for the credit channel. Each is a follow-up project.

Methodological notes

Data pipeline

40 Orbis Excel exports (3,058 firms each) parsed via direct XML. Year-by-variable column map for the 2011 to 2014 loans block. Cross-batch dedup by BvD ID, firm age from Excel serial dates, wide-to-long reshape with ICR, ROA, investment rate, negative-equity flag, and NACE 2-digit derived once. 351,220 firm-years after the filters.

Regression and spatial methods

linearmodels PanelOLS, firm and year fixed effects, SEs clustered at province-by-2-digit-NACE. Continuous controls winsorised at the 1 percent tails. Leave-one-out congestion is computed per cell; the IDW measure uses GeoNames CAP centroids with a row-normalised inverse-distance kernel within each bandwidth.

ML protocol

Time-based split: train 2016 to 2021, validation 2022, test 2023. Class weighting for the 1-to-21 imbalance. Logistic, Random Forest, XGBoost compared on AUPRC (primary, appropriate under heavy imbalance), ROC-AUC and F1 (secondary). Two specs run side by side: with ICR features (leaky baseline) and without (the reported result).

Code, data, and reproducibility

Six Jupyter notebooks under code/ cover the full pipeline (merge, classification, geographic analysis, regression, charts, ML). Orbis raw exports cannot be redistributed under the LSE licence; docs/ records the search criteria, saved view, and column-level handling so any Orbis user can rebuild the panel.
