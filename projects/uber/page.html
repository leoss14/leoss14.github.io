Spatial and platform analysis

How NYC's App-Based For-Hire Market Changed, 2018 to 2026

An analysis of how the NYC app-based ride-hailing market evolved between February 2019 and April 2026, using public Taxi and Limousine Commission trip records. The page documents the spatial redistribution of pickup demand, the rise of platform per-trip margin, and the response to the January 2025 congestion fee, on a stratified random sample of 8.34 million trips.

Introduction

What Eight Years of Trip Records Show

App-based ride-hailing handles the majority of for-hire trips in New York City and supports tens of thousands of drivers in primary or supplementary work. The two operators in this market, Uber and Lyft, run proprietary dispatch and pricing systems whose internal decisions are not publicly disclosed; their observable consequences, however, are visible in the NYC Taxi and Limousine Commission's released trip records. This page uses those records to ask how the market evolved between February 2019 and April 2026, and whether the joint pattern of observed changes points to a single underlying mechanism. The trip-level analysis runs on a stratified random sample of 8.34 million trips.

Between 2019 and 2026, the geographic distribution of pickups spread out, with the outer boroughs growing in demand, while the core Manhattan area market shrank. This coincided with an increasing operator margin: while in March 2019 the typical Uber trip ran at a small loss to the platform, the revenue-weighted operator margin stabilised in the low-20% range. The page also includes a brief on the January 2025 congestion-fee shock, testing whether imposing a $1.50 per-trip toll on high-volume for-hire trips would lead to any visible behavioural break in either rider demand or driver pay. The final section returns to whether a common mechanism (algorithmic dispatch optimisation, post-pandemic remote work, the 2025 legislation, or some combination of them) plausibly accounts for the experienced market evolution.

Where pickups moved

Geography of FHV Demand

The borough markets don't perfectly match the FHV market: Upper Manhattan, for example, behaves more like the Bronx than like the rest of Manhattan. We use four clusters fit to the trip data as an intermediate unit.

To identify demand areas, we use the zones' geographic coordinates (latitude and longitude to define geographically connected areas), as well as each zone's origin-destination profile, meaning the share of its pickups going to every other zone and the share of its arrivals coming from every other zone. This produces some isolates, like LaGuardia included as a part of Manhattan, as most of its trips originate or end up in Manhattan. Ignoring geographical position would generate similar results, with JFK being included as a part of Manhattan. To ensure compactness, we use both measures, but alternative specifications and technical details around the clustering process are included in the appendix.

The four clusters and their share of total panel pickups are: Manhattan (39%), Brooklyn (27%, plus Staten Island), Bronx + Upper Manhattan (18%), and Queens (17%). Every per-zone metric in the rest of this page (margin, response time, dispatch wait, Pool share, tip rate, driver pay) is aggregated to one of these four clusters.

Four demand regions, fit on the full 2019 to 2026 panel

Each TLC zone is shaded by its assigned cluster. Borough outlines drawn in black. Hover a zone for its name, borough, total pickup volume across the panel, and cluster.

The share of pickups per cluster has changed over time, with the Manhattan cluster's share of monthly Uber pickups falling from 42% in 2019 to 37% in 2026, while the Queens cluster gaining the most (15% to 19%). The other two stay approximately flat. Most of the change came as a result of the pandemic shock in 2020, redistributing share to the outer clusters. While the share rebounded toward Manhattan through 2022, the downward Manhattan drift then resumed from 2022 onward. The redistribution concentrated at cluster cores: Manhattan's losses were largest in Midtown and Queens's gains were largest in deep Queens, not at the boundaries between clusters (see the appendix).

Geography also shapes which trips the platform absorbs losses on. A trip runs at a negative margin when Uber pays the driver more than the rider's base fare, typically because driver utilisation in that area is low enough (long dispatch waits, long deadhead miles between trips) that the platform must subsidise the trip to keep supply available. In the Manhattan cluster, where demand is densest and dispatch waits shortest, the negative-margin share fell from 31% of trips in 2019 to under 10% from 2022 onward. In the outer clusters the share fell during the 2020 to 2022 repricing but began rising again from 2023, and by 2026 the Bronx + Upper Manhattan cluster runs more than a quarter of its Uber trips at negative margin.

Per-trip margin

The Platform's Take Rate Rose

To observe the profitability of FHV ridesharing service between 2019 and 2026, we use the following proxy: the rider's base fare minus driver pay, divided by the rider's base fare. Positive values mean the platform retained some share of the fare; negative values mean it paid the driver more than the rider's base fare and absorbed the difference. A side effect of this is that the proxy excludes tolls, taxes, congestion surcharges, and tips, since none of these flow to the platform as retained revenue (tolls and surcharges pass to authorities, tips go to drivers). Across the panel, the operator margin has greatly increased. March 2019 is the only month with a negative revenue-weighted margin (-4.8%); 58.5% of trips that month ran at negative margin. Between March and April 2020 the revenue-weighted line jumped from +8.6% to +21.3% as the short low-margin trips were made unprofitable by the minimum-pay rule introduced in 2020, with the revenue-weighted margin hovering in the low 20s thereafter.

The revenue-weighted line is one minus total monthly driver pay divided by total monthly base fare, which weights every dollar of fare equally and is what a corporate take rate would look like. Dotted vertical lines mark the regulatory events listed below.

The margin recovery was not uniform across the city. In early 2019 the four clusters operated at very different margins, with a roughly 15-percentage-point spread between the Manhattan cluster (the highest) and the Bronx + Upper Manhattan cluster (the lowest). The April 2020 minimum-pay rule and the pandemic-era loss of short low-margin trips compressed all four lines to roughly 20% by August 2020. They tracked together through early 2023, when the Manhattan cluster began pulling away. By 2026 the Manhattan cluster sits around 30%, Brooklyn and Queens around 17%, and Bronx + Upper Manhattan visibly lower at 11 to 13%.

The composition of profitable trips also reversed across the panel. In 2019, the median trip in every distance bucket except the longest ran at a loss to the platform (under 3 miles: -3%; 3 to 6 miles: -9%; 6 to 12 miles: -9%; over 12 miles: +4%). By 2025 the pattern had flipped: short trips (under 3 miles) became the highest-margin bucket at roughly 28%, while longer trips converged around 12 to 15%. The minimum-pay rule raised the driver-pay floor on short trips, and the platform responded by raising short-trip fares enough to expand its own retained margin rather than absorb the cost.

Uber vs Lyft, head to head

Uber vs Lyft, Trip by Trip

Parts 1 and 2 used aggregates: one monthly number per operator. The differences between Uber and Lyft do not show up at that level of detail. This part uses a stratified random sample of 8.34 million individual trips (4.98M Uber, 3.36M Lyft) covering February 2019 to April 2026; sampling weights restore unbiased population-level estimates. The four geographic clusters from Part 1 (Manhattan, Brooklyn, Bronx + Upper Manhattan, Queens) are used throughout.

Across the full panel, Uber's revenue-weighted margin averaged 19.7% and Lyft's averaged 21.7%. The two-percentage-point gap is small but the trajectory underneath it is not: Uber started 2019 deep underwater (−5% in March 2019, the only negative-margin month for either platform in the panel) and climbed quickly to converge with Lyft by 2022. From 2024 onward the two lines track each other closely. The cleaner statement is that Lyft retained more per dollar than Uber for most of the panel's first half, the two converged in 2022, and they have run roughly parallel since.

January 2025 policy shock

The Congestion-Fee Shock

On 5 January 2025, the MTA's Central Business District Tolling Program (the Congestion Relief Zone, commonly called congestion pricing) took effect: vehicles entering Manhattan south of and including 60th Street became subject to a per-trip toll. For high-volume for-hire vehicles (Uber and Lyft), the fee is $1.50 per CBD trip, applied uniformly across peak and overnight periods to any trip that starts, ends, or passes through the zone. Under the toll schedule adopted by the MTA Board, the $1.50 rate is 60% of the originally approved $2.50, following Governor Hochul's November 2024 plan that cut all CBD tolls by 40%; the schedule then raises the rate to 80% in 2028 and 100% in 2031. The fee is statutorily collected from the passenger and remitted to the MTA, so by law the platforms cannot absorb it themselves. In this section, the policy zones (CBD, the 60th-to-65th-Street buffer, and the rest of the city) are used to observe the effects of the policy.

Total monthly trip volume across the city kept rising through 2025 and 2026, with no visible drop at the fee date. Absolute CBD volume is roughly flat in the months around January 2025, but absolute levels are confounded by steady upward drift in total ride-hailing demand. Levels alone do not establish whether the fee changed rider behaviour; the share-based charts below offer a cleaner test.

Scaled to operator-level shares, the data does not show a sharp discontinuity at the fee date. Both Uber's and Lyft's CBD pickup share began declining in early 2024, a full year before the fee, falling about 4 percentage points between then and mid-2026. The post-fee period continues that gradual decline rather than breaking from it. A discrete policy effect would appear as a step down in January 2025; the chart does not show one. The trend is consistent with reduced post-pandemic CBD demand more than with a fee-driven substitution.

If riders shifted Manhattan pickups north to avoid the cordon, the 60th-to-65th-Street buffer is where the substitution would show. The buffer's share of Manhattan pickups has hovered in an 8.5-to-9.5% band for years, with substantial monthly noise. No discernible step occurs at the January 2025 fee date; the post-fee months stay within the same band as the years preceding it. There is no evidence of rider substitution into the buffer.

The remaining question concerns the fare components. The fee itself is collected from the passenger by statute, so its passthrough to the rider's total bill is mechanical, not a platform decision. What the platforms can choose is whether to also change the underlying base fare (the price Uber and Lyft set, which excludes the fee, tolls, taxes, and tips). The chart below shows monthly mean base fare per trip for CBD trips and outer-borough trips, separately. The CBD base-fare line is volatile, with month-to-month swings of several dollars throughout the panel; the visible vertical shift at the fee date sits within that volatility band rather than above it. Driver pay shows no comparable change in either zone.

Base fare and driver pay per trip, CBD versus outer boroughs

Two-panel chart. Left: mean base passenger fare per trip (USD), CBD trips (rose) versus outer-borough trips (navy), monthly. Right: mean driver pay per trip, same colours and zones. Both panels mark 5 January 2025 with a dashed line. Base fare excludes the congestion fee, tolls, taxes, surcharges, and tips by construction.

We attempted to estimate the fee's causal effect on base fares with a difference-in-differences design, comparing trips with pickup or dropoff in the CBD (treated) against trips with neither end in the CBD (control), across pre and post windows around January 2025. The design does not identify a clean effect. A canonical event-study regression with pickup-zone and calendar-month fixed effects, run on log base fare with leads and lags from −12 to +12 months around the policy, rejects the parallel-trends assumption decisively: F = 37.1 for Uber and F = 14.1 for Lyft, both with p < 0.001 (against an F threshold of ~1.8 at the 5% level). Treated-zone fares were already drifting upward relative to control fares well before January 2025; the post-period coefficients reflect that continuing trend rather than a discrete policy break. Two coefficient estimates can be read from the data: a tight-window static DiD (pre = Sept–Dec 2024, post = Feb–May 2025) returns −$0.31 per trip on Uber base fare and −$1.64 per trip on Lyft, while a wider 12-month-window log specification returns +16% (Uber) and +9% (Lyft). The two windows give opposite signs because the trend is monotonic and the choice of comparison window dominates the result. Beyond the failed pre-trends, the design has a second identification problem: the policy is market-wide, so non-CBD trips are not a clean control (substitution, driver redistribution, and equilibrium repricing all propagate outward), violating the stable-unit-treatment-value assumption. The chart below shows the descriptive monthly fare trajectories that motivated the DiD; the canonical event-study coefficient plot in the appendix shows the diagnostic that rejected it. The defensible interpretation of the data is that the fee was passed through to riders by statute as designed, and that neither operator made a visible structural adjustment to their underlying base fare around the policy date.

Taken together, the five charts give a consistent reading: the fee passed through to riders by statutory requirement, but neither platform used the policy event as cover to raise base fares above the fee. Rider behaviour also did not visibly change: CBD trip volume continued its pre-existing trajectory, the gradual CBD share decline that began in early 2024 did not accelerate at the fee date, and the buffer zone did not absorb diverted demand. The data is consistent with low short-run price elasticity of demand for CBD ride-hailing trips in the post-fee year, though longer-run adjustments (including the scheduled phase-in to 80% in 2028 and 100% in 2031) may unfold beyond the available data window.

Interpretation

What These Patterns Could Mean

The five preceding parts described what changed: the Uber-Lyft duopoly tilting toward Uber, geographic redistribution of pickup demand, the rising platform take rate, the head-to-head Uber/Lyft picture from a trip-level sample, and near-complete passthrough of the January 2025 congestion fee. This part asks what could explain those patterns together. The descriptive findings stand on their own; the interpretation in this section is one reading consistent with the data, not the only reading.

Ride-hailing platforms decide which driver gets assigned to each incoming passenger request. Two approaches sit at opposite ends of the design spectrum, and each implies a different spatial footprint.

Greedy matching assigns the closest available driver to each request, minimising immediate pickup time. The rule is simple but myopic: it ignores where the driver ends up after the trip. A driver two minutes away in Midtown might take a fare to an isolated area, earning well on that single ride but then waiting thirty minutes for the next one.

MDP-based matching takes the opposite approach. The Markov Decision Process framework documented in Xu et al. (2018) maximises long-run system value rather than immediate pickup time. The algorithm learns a value for each geographic zone at each time of day, built from historical trip observations. Each dispatch decision evaluates the immediate fare plus the change in the driver's future position value:

Dispatch decision rule

$$\Delta V(s, s') = \text{Fare}(s, s') + V(s') - V(s)$$

The algorithm picks the assignment that maximises \(\Delta V\): the immediate fare for the trip from origin state \(s\) to destination state \(s'\), plus the future position value gained at \(s'\) net of the current position value at \(s\). A state here is a (zone, time-of-day) pair.

The spatial implication is that MDP-style matching should redistribute supply toward zones with high future value (airports especially, since they position drivers for high-fare return trips into Manhattan) and away from zones where drivers are already saturated. Over time, this should produce a more even demand surface, lower spatial clustering of high-activity neighbourhoods, and more trips completed locally as drivers are matched to nearby riders more efficiently. Xu et al. (2018) report that MDP-based dispatch improved trip completion rates by 0.5% to 5% across more than twenty cities relative to a greedy baseline.

Three patterns in the panel are broadly consistent with this kind of spatial redistribution. None is dispositive on its own, but together they sketch the geography a value-based dispatch system would tend to produce.

The first is the airport share increase. Combined JFK, LaGuardia, and Newark pickup share rose roughly 48% over the panel. Airports are the canonical high-future-value zone in a value-based system: a driver completing a drop-off at JFK is well-positioned for a high-fare return trip into Manhattan, so the algorithm should push drivers toward airport pickups whenever possible. The directional change is large and consistent with this prediction, though airport ground-transportation policy changed over the same window (terminal access restrictions for non-app hails tilted the playing field toward app-dispatched operators independent of any dispatch logic), so part of the change is plausibly attributable to policy rather than algorithm.

The second is the decline in the Manhattan core. The top three single zones by pickup volume each lost 34 to 39% of their share of total monthly Uber pickups across the panel. A value-based system should spread activity away from saturated zones, and the Manhattan core is by far the most saturated zone in this dataset. The decline is broadly consistent across multiple central zones rather than concentrated in one anomalous location. Post-pandemic remote work is an equally plausible explanation that cannot be ruled out: Manhattan office occupancy in 2025 remains substantially below 2019 levels, which would reduce commuter-style trips into the CBD independent of any dispatch decision.

The third is the fall in spatial autocorrelation. Moran's I on monthly pickup intensity dropped from 0.55 to 0.28 across the panel (both estimates significant at p < 0.001). Moran's I measures whether nearby zones have similar activity levels: high values mean hot zones cluster geographically, low values mean the activity surface is smoother. The near-halving is consistent with the prediction that MDP-style dispatch makes the demand surface more even. Of the three findings, this is the most robust to volume confounds: Moran's I is a correlation measure rather than a concentration measure, so it is not mechanically affected by the growth in total trip volume that occurred over the panel.

If dispatch optimisation does what the MDP framework predicts (longer trips, less driver idle time, denser matches), drivers should earn more per worked mile and per worked hour without much movement in pay per trip. The pay structure across the panel matches this pattern in nominal terms. Adjusting for CPI inflation tells a different story: real pay per hour in February 2019 dollars is roughly $50 at the start and roughly $50 at the end, with the 2020 minimum-pay rule producing the only durable level shift. The post-2022 nominal gains are largely inflation. Two caveats on the per-hour series: it uses passenger time only (pickup to dropoff), so time spent cruising, waiting, or dispatched but not yet at pickup is not in the denominator, and true earnings per online hour are lower than the line shown. Independent studies have estimated NYC driver utilisation rates of roughly 50 to 70 percent of online time.

Nominal pay per trip stepped up at the February 2019 minimum-pay rule and has stayed in a tight band around $20 since 2021. Pay per mile rose more continuously, from $3.20 in 2019 to $4.30 in 2026 (+34%). The two together describe a fleet doing similar numbers of trips at similar headline pay but accumulating more miles per worked hour: the pattern a dispatch system optimised for utilisation would produce. The driver-share-of-fare chart says the same thing from a different angle: drivers took home more than 100% of the base fare in early 2019 (the platform was subsidising trips to absorb the minimum-pay rule), stepped down at the April 2020 plateau, and have sat in a 75 to 82% range since. Per-trip earnings stayed roughly similar in nominal terms while platform retention rose, because fares rose faster than pay.

The dispatch-algorithm reading is consistent with the descriptive patterns, but the patterns are equally consistent with at least four other explanations that cannot be ruled out from public TLC data alone:

  • Remote work: The post-pandemic decline in Manhattan office occupancy reduces commuter-style trips into the CBD independent of any dispatch decision. This can explain the falling Manhattan share without invoking the algorithm.
  • Airport ground-transportation policy: Terminal access restrictions for non-app for-hire pickups at JFK and LaGuardia tilted the playing field toward app-dispatched operators over the panel window, raising Uber's airport share for reasons unrelated to dispatch optimisation.
  • Pricing: The platform's own pricing changes (variable surge, upfront pricing, the post-2020 short-trip repricing documented in Part 2) directly shape which trips happen and where, independent of how drivers are matched.
  • Schema change: Uber's pre-Feb 2019 trip records use the older FHV reporting schema. Cross-period comparisons that look like algorithmic effects may partly reflect changes in what fields are reported and how zones are coded.

Distinguishing these explanations would require a controlled experiment, a regression discontinuity around a documented algorithm-deployment date, or detailed data on operator pricing decisions. None of those is available with public TLC data. The patterns in this page are consistent with the MDP framework, but they cannot identify it as the unique explanation.

Limitations

What This Analysis Does Not Resolve

Algorithm opacity

Uber's actual dispatch algorithm is proprietary; neither the matching policy, the surge formula, nor the geographic targeting layer is publicly documented at the level needed to verify what is deployed at any given month. The Markov decision process framework discussed in Part 5 is taken from Xu et al. (2018), a research paper authored by engineers at Didi Chuxing that has been widely cited as the canonical academic description of value-based dispatch in large ride-hailing platforms. The framework predicts that an optimising dispatcher will produce longer trips on average, smoother spatial demand, lower idle-driver time, and a divergence between pay per worked hour and pay per online hour. This page tests those qualitative predictions against observable patterns in the public TLC data. It cannot confirm which specific algorithm Uber actually runs, when it was deployed, how it has been retuned since, or how its parameters differ across boroughs or time of day. Several non-algorithmic explanations (the 2020 minimum-pay rule, the pandemic shock, post-2020 Manhattan office vacancy, the TLC's 2023 pool-relaunch ruling, and the gradual shift in the FHV fleet toward higher-utilisation drivers) could also generate similar patterns; the page argues only that the observed data is consistent with the MDP prediction, not that it identifies it.

Driver-level data are not available

The TLC trip records do not include driver identifiers. The Gini on driver pay per trip is therefore dispersion across trips, not across drivers. A driver who works 40 hours per week earning per-trip averages will have annual earnings that differ in unknown ways from the per-trip distribution.

Margin proxy is a simplification

The operator margin proxy ((base fare - driver pay) / base fare) excludes tolls, taxes, congestion surcharges, and tips. It captures the platform's contribution margin on the base fare component but not its overall take rate, which would also include the fees the platform charges drivers (rental, leasing) and the operational costs the platform bears (matching, customer service, infrastructure).

CBD identification limits

The Part 4 congestion-fee analysis uses approximately one year of post-fee data, and the canonical event-study specification (in the appendix) rejects parallel pre-trends between inside-CBD and outside-CBD trips. The DiD design therefore cannot identify a clean causal effect; the section interprets the data descriptively. Strategic adjustments by platforms or riders may also unfold on longer horizons than the available window.

Appendix

Methodology and Supplementary Material

Part 1 supporting charts: spatial diagnostics

The charts below support the prose in Part 1. They cover descriptive measures of how the spatial distribution of pickups changed: the Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient (concentration), Moran's I and the local LISA decomposition (spatial polarisation), zone-level pickup density change, and borough-level mix. All point in the same direction (less concentrated, less polarised); none changes the substantive reading. Note that these charts compare January 2018 to January 2025 single-month snapshots rather than using the full 87-month panel.

Clustering method and OD-only robustness check

The clustering algorithm is K-means with K = 4. K-means partitions a set of points into K groups such that points within a group are close in feature space and far from points in other groups. The features are each zone's geographic centre point (latitude and longitude) standardised, concatenated with the first eight principal components of its origin-destination profile, also standardised. Each zone is weighted by its total pickup volume across the 87-month panel (February 2019 to April 2026), so high-volume zones influence cluster boundaries proportional to the trips they generate. The clustering is fit once on the full panel and used consistently across the rest of the page.

As a robustness check, the map below shows the alternative that uses only the OD profile, with no geographic features. The two clusterings agree on 90% of zones by pickup volume. The visible disagreements are JFK (assigned to Manhattan here because its trips are almost entirely Manhattan-bound), Bay Ridge and Borough Park in southern Brooklyn (assigned to Queens because their dropoff patterns favour Queens destinations), and several other zones near borough boundaries. The main-body clustering is preferred because spatial contiguity makes the per-cluster results easier to interpret.

Where the share change came from: core zones vs cluster edges

For each zone we compute its distance from the cluster center, defined as the ratio of its distance to its own cluster's volume-weighted centroid over the sum of that distance and the distance to the nearest other cluster's centroid. A value near 0 means the zone sits deep inside its own cluster; a value near 0.5 means it sits on the boundary with another cluster. We then ask whether the 2019-to-2026 share change (in percentage points of total Uber pickups) correlates with distance within each cluster. Use the buttons to switch between clusters.

Zone-level share change vs distance from cluster center, by cluster

Each circle is one zone, sized by total pickup volume across the panel. The Manhattan cluster shows a positive correlation (ρ = +0.48, p < 0.001): zones close to the cluster centroid (low distance) lost the most share (East Village, Union Sq, Penn Station, Midtown); zones near the cluster edge lost less or gained (LIC, Greenpoint, LaGuardia). The Queens cluster shows the mirror image (ρ = −0.32, p = 0.010): the cluster's core gained most (Jamaica, South Ozone Park, Forest Hills), and the zones near the Manhattan border (Steinway, LIC/Queens Plaza) lost slightly. Brooklyn and Bronx + Upper Manhattan show no significant pattern, consistent with their cluster-level shares being approximately flat over the panel.

Concentration: Lorenz curves and Gini

The Gini coefficient summarises how unevenly pickups are distributed across the 263 zones (0 = perfect equality across zones, 1 = full concentration in one zone). The Lorenz curve plots cumulative pickup share against cumulative zone share ranked low to high; a perfectly even distribution traces the diagonal and the further the curve bows below it, the more concentrated demand is.

Lorenz curves, 2018 versus 2025

Gini fell from 0.511 (2018) to 0.440 (2025). Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals (1,000 resamples of zone-level counts) are non-overlapping: 2018 [0.475, 0.541], 2025 [0.402, 0.473].

Spatial polarisation: Moran's I and LISA

Moran's I measures whether high-demand zones cluster together geographically. The spatial weights matrix uses each zone's six nearest neighbours by centroid distance, row-standardised. Global Moran's I fell from 0.55 in 2018 to 0.28 in 2025 (both p < 0.001 under a 999-permutation null). LISA decomposes the global statistic into zone-level labels: hot spot (high near high), cold spot (low near low), spatial outlier (high near low or vice versa), or not significant.

Zone-level trip counts were resampled 1,000 times with replacement, and the Gini coefficient recomputed on each iteration. The resulting distributions for 2018 and 2025 do not overlap at the 95% level, confirming that the observed decline is not a sampling artifact.

Pickup and dropoff balance

The pickup-to-dropoff ratio per zone, defined as PU / (PU + DO), measures whether a zone is a net origin or destination. A value of 0.5 indicates perfect balance. The Kolmogorov-Smirnov two-sample test detected a significant distributional shift between 2018 and 2025 (D = 0.21, p < 0.001), while Levene's test found no change in variance (p = 0.95): the distribution moved, but its spread did not change.

Concepts and definitions

K-means clustering. An unsupervised algorithm that partitions a set of points into K groups by minimising the sum of squared distances from each point to the centroid of its assigned group. Used here with K = 4 on each zone's geographic centre point (latitude and longitude) standardised, concatenated with the first eight principal components of its origin-destination profile, also standardised. Each zone is weighted by total pickup volume across the 87-month panel. The same clustering is used consistently throughout the page.

Distance from cluster center. For each zone, the ratio of its distance to its own cluster's volume-weighted centroid over the sum of that distance and the distance to the nearest other cluster's centroid. A value near 0 means the zone sits deep inside its cluster; a value near 0.5 means it sits on the boundary with another cluster. Used to test whether share changes were concentrated at cluster cores or edges.

Spearman rank correlation. A non-parametric measure of monotonic association between two ranked variables. Range is [-1, 1]; a value of +1 means perfect rank concordance, -1 means perfect rank discordance, 0 means no monotonic relationship. Used here to test whether zone-level share change correlates with distance from cluster centroid.

Gini coefficient and Lorenz curve. The Lorenz curve plots the cumulative share of pickups against the cumulative share of zones, ranked from lowest to highest demand. The Gini coefficient is twice the area between the Lorenz curve and the diagonal: it ranges from 0 (perfectly equal distribution) to 1 (all activity in a single zone). Originally a measure of income inequality, applied here to demand inequality across taxi zones and to driver pay per trip.

Moran's I. A statistic measuring spatial autocorrelation: the degree to which nearby zones have similar values. Range is roughly [-1, 1]; a value close to +1 indicates strong positive autocorrelation (high near high, low near low), zero indicates a random spatial pattern, and negative values indicate checkerboard-like dispersion. Both years are statistically significant at p < 0.001 under a permutation null, so the change is not noise.

LISA (Local Indicators of Spatial Association). The local decomposition of Moran's I. Each zone receives a label based on whether it is high or low and whether its neighbours are high or low: HH (hot spot), LL (cold spot), HL or LH (spatial outliers), or not significant. Used here to identify where the spatial polarisation contracted.

Operator margin proxy. Per trip, defined as (base passenger fare minus driver pay) divided by base passenger fare. Captures what the platform retains on the base fare component before tolls, taxes, congestion surcharges, and tips. Aggregated three ways: trip-level mean, median, and revenue-weighted (sum of fares minus sum of pay, divided by sum of fares).

Markov Decision Process (MDP). A mathematical framework for sequential decision-making under uncertainty. A state describes the current situation (here, a zone and time-of-day pair), an action transitions the system to a new state, and the optimal policy chooses actions that maximise the expected sum of immediate rewards plus the value of future states. Xu et al. (2018) frame ride-hailing dispatch as an MDP and learn the zone-time value function from historical trip data.

Haversine distance. The great-circle distance between two points on a sphere, given their latitude and longitude. Used here for the K-nearest-neighbour spatial weights underlying Moran's I and LISA, and for the volume-weighted cluster centroid computation in the edge-vs-core analysis.

Methodology and estimation

Data

NYC TLC public trip records in Parquet format. The two-period geographic comparison uses January 2018 (19.8M total FHV trips: 4.5M Uber, 3.1M Lyft, 12.2M other FHV) and January 2025 (20.4M total FHV trips: 15.4M Uber, 5.0M Lyft, 0 other). The full margin panel uses every monthly FHVHV file from February 2019 through April 2026 (roughly 1.12 billion Uber trips). Uber identified by dispatching base number in 2018 and by HVFHS licence number HV0003 from February 2019 onward. Zone polygons and centroids come from the NYC Open Data shapefile of 263 TLC taxi zones.

Geographic methods

K-means clustering with K = 4 (random_state = 42, ten initialisations) on standardised geographic coordinates (latitude, longitude) concatenated with the first eight principal components of each zone's origin-destination profile, also standardised. Each zone is weighted by its total pickup volume across the 87-month panel. Clustering is fit once on the full panel and used consistently across Parts 1 through 5. The OD-only robustness check uses the same K but drops the geographic features. Gini coefficient computed by trapezoidal integration under the Lorenz curve; bootstrap 95% intervals from 1,000 resamples of zone-level trip counts.

Spatial methods

Global Moran's I and local LISA computed with PySAL/ESDA, using K-nearest-neighbour spatial weights with k = 6 and row standardisation. Permutation p-values from the standard 999-permutation null implemented in ESDA. Kolmogorov-Smirnov two-sample test for the pickup/dropoff mismatch ratio distribution; Levene's test for variance equality of the same distribution. The edge-vs-core test uses Spearman rank correlation between zone-level share change and distance from cluster centroid, reported with the asymptotic p-value.

Margin methods

Per-trip margin proxy computed in DuckDB directly over the parquet files, with trip-level filters: base passenger fare in (1, 500), driver pay greater than 0.50, trip miles in (0.1, 100), trip time in (30, 18000) seconds. Monthly aggregation produces revenue-weighted, trip-mean, and median series; quantile percentiles via t-digest (DuckDB approx_quantile) for the fan envelope. The revenue-weighted margin definition (sum of platform-retained dollars over sum of rider-paid dollars) is preferred for operator-level comparisons because it matches the industry definition of platform take rate.

Trip-level sample

Parts 3 and 4 use a stratified random sample of 8.34 million Uber and Lyft trips (4.98M Uber, 3.36M Lyft) covering February 2019 to April 2026. Strata are defined by month, operator, and pickup zone class; within each stratum, trips are drawn uniformly at random with sampling rates that produce roughly comparable cell sizes. Sampling weights (the inverse probability of selection) restore unbiased population-level estimates when aggregated. All trip-level statistics in Parts 3 and 4 are weighted.

CBD passthrough methods

Difference-in-differences on Uber base passenger fare (which excludes the congestion fee, tolls, taxes, surcharges, and tips by construction). Treatment trips are those with pickup OR dropoff in the CBD (Manhattan zones with centroid latitude at or below 40.7644 degrees, the 60th-Street threshold, excluding Roosevelt, Randalls, Governors, Liberty, and Ellis Islands). Control trips have neither end in the CBD. Pre window is September to December 2024; post window is February to May 2025. January 2025 is excluded because the fee took effect on 5 January (partial-month treatment) and because Lyft credited the $1.50 to passengers throughout that month. Estimation is weighted OLS using the trip-level sampling weights, with HC1 robust standard errors. Run separately for Uber and Lyft; results in main body. Reported with both level (USD per trip) and log specifications.
